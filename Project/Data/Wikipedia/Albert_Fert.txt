Currently, he is an emeritus professor at [[Paris-Saclay University]] in [[Orsay]], scientific director of a joint laboratory (''Unit\u00e9 mixte de recherche'') between the [[Centre national de la recherche scientifique]] (National Scientific Research Centre) and [[Thales Group]], and adjunct professor at [[Michigan State University]]. He was awarded the 2007 [[Nobel Prize in Physics]] together with [[Peter Gr\u00fcnberg]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/physics/laureates/2007/press.html|title=The Nobel Prize in Physics 2007}}</ref>\n\n==Biography==\nIn 1962 Albert Fert graduated from the [[\u00c9cole Normale Sup\u00e9rieure]] in [[Paris]],<ref name=\":1\">{{Cite web|title=Prof. Albert Fert {{!}} GSE Mainz|url=https://www.mainz.uni-mainz.de/prof-albert-fert/|access-date=2021-01-19|website=www.mainz.uni-mainz.de}}</ref> where he attended courses by the physicists [[Alfred Kastler]] and [[Jacques Friedel]].  (As an undergraduate he had strong interests in photography and cinema, and was a great admirer of the work of [[Ingmar Bergman]]).)<ref>{{Cite web|title=Albert Fert, un Nobel amoureux de Bergman|url=https://www.lefigaro.fr/sciences/2007/12/31/01008-20071231ARTFIG00196-albert-fert-un-nobel-amoureux-de-bergman.php|access-date=2021-01-19|website=LEFIGARO|date=31 December 2007 |language=fr}}</ref>\n\nAfter the \u00c9cole Normale Sup\u00e9rieure, Fert attended the [[University of Grenoble]] and in 1963 received his Ph.D. (''doctorat de troisi\u00e8me cycle'') from the [[University of Paris]] with a thesis prepared in the fundamental electronic [[Paris-Saclay Faculty of Sciences|Orsay Faculty of Sciences]] and in the physical spectrometry laboratory of the University of Grenoble Faculty of Sciences.\n\nOn his return from military service in 1965, Fert became assistant professor at the Orsay Faculty of Sciences of the [[University of Paris XI]] ([[Universit\u00e9 Paris-Sud]]).<ref>{{Cite web|last=|first=|date=|title=The Nobel Prize in Physics 2007 \u2013 Albert Fert \u2013 Facts|url=https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2007/fert/facts/|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2021-01-19|website=NobelPrize.org|language=en-US}}</ref> Under the direction of Ian Campbell at the [[Laboratoire de Physique des Solides|Laboratory of Solid Physics]] he prepared for a doctorate Sc.D. (''doctorat des sciences'') in Physical Sciences on the electrical transport properties of [[nickel]] and [[iron]], which he completed in 1970.<ref name=\":1\" /> He was named professor in 1976.\n\nFert worked as research director for the university's [[Condensed matter physics|condensed-matter physics]] laboratory (1970\u20131995) prior to moving to Unit\u00e9 Mixte de Physique, a laboratory jointly run by the Universit\u00e9 Paris-Sud and the technology company [[Thales Group|Thales]].\n\nIn 1988, Albert Fert at Orsay in France, and Peter Gruenberg at J\u00fclich in Germany, simultaneously and independently discovered [[giant magnetoresistance]] (GMR) in magnetic multilayers.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Baibich|first1=M. In 2007, Fert and Prof. Gr\u00fcnberg jointly received the [[Japan Prize|Japan Award]] (300.000 Euro) for their discovery of GMR. \n\nIn October 2006, Professor Fert received an honorary doctorate from the Department of Physics of the [[Technical University of Kaiserslautern|University of Kaiserslautern]].<ref name=\":1\" />\n\nFert has made many contributions to the development of spintronics.
