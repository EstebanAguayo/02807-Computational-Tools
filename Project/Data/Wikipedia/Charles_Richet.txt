{"batchcomplete": "", "query": {"normalized": [{"from": "Charles_Richet", "to": "Charles Richet"}], "pages": {"301758": {"pageid": 301758, "ns": 0, "title": "Charles Richet", "revisions": [{"slots": {"main": {"contentmodel": "wikitext", "contentformat": "text/x-wiki", "*": "{{short description|French physiologist}}\n{{Use dmy dates|date=August 2021}}\n{{Infobox scientist\n|image      = Charles Robert Richet 3.jpg\n|image_size =\n|caption    =\n|birth_date = {{birth date|1850|8|25|df=y}}\n|birth_place= Paris, France\n|death_date = {{death date and age|1935|12|4|1850|8|25|df=y}}\n|death_place= Paris, France\n|alma_mater = [[University of Paris]]\n|prizes     = [[Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine]] (1913)\n}}\n\n'''Charles Robert Richet''' (25 August 1850 \u2013 4 December 1935) was a French [[Physiology|physiologist]] at the [[Coll\u00e8ge de France]] known for his pioneering work in [[immunology]]. In 1913, he won the [[Nobel Prize]] in Physiology or Medicine \"in recognition of his work on [[anaphylaxis]]\".<ref name=\"nobel\">{{cite web |title=The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine 1913 Charles Richet |url=http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/medicine/laureates/1913/index.html |access-date=5 July 2010 |publisher=Nobelprize.org}}</ref> Richet devoted many years to the study of [[paranormal]] and [[spiritualism|spiritualist]] phenomena, coining the term \"[[Ectoplasm (paranormal)|ectoplasm]]\". He also believed in the inferiority of Black people, was a proponent of [[eugenics]] and presided over the French Eugenics Society towards the end of his life. The Richet line of professorships of medical science would continue through his son Charles and his grandson Gabriel.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Dworetzky|first1=Murray|last2=Cohen|first2=Sheldon|last3=Cohen|first3=Sheldon G.|last4=Zelaya-Quesada|first4=Myrna|date=August 2002|title=Portier, Richet, and the discovery of anaphylaxis: A centennial|journal=Journal of Allergy and Clinical Immunology|volume=110|issue=2|pages=331\u2013336|doi=10.1016/S0091-6749(02)70118-8|pmid=12170279 }}</ref> Gabriel Richet was one of the great pioneers of European [[nephrology]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Piccoli|first1=Giorgina Barbara|last2=Richiero|first2=Gilberto|last3=Jaar|first3=Bernard G.|date=13 March 2018|title=The Pioneers of Nephrology \u2013 Professor Gabriel Richet: \"I will maintain\"|journal=BMC Nephrology|volume=19|issue=1|pages=60|doi=10.1186/s12882-018-0862-0|issn=1471-2369|pmc=5851327|pmid=29534697}}</ref>\n\n==Career==\n[[File:Charles Richet 1922.jpg|thumb|Richet in 1922]]\nHe was born on 25 August 1850 in Paris the son of Alfred Richet. He was educated at the Lycee Bonaparte in Paris then studied Medicine at university in Paris.<ref>{{cite book|title=Biographical Index of Former Fellows of the Royal Society of Edinburgh 1783\u20132002|date=July 2006|publisher=The Royal Society of Edinburgh|isbn=0-902-198-84-X|url=https://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp2.pdf|access-date=19 March 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160304074135/https://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp2.pdf|archive-date=4 March 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref>\n\nRichet spent a period of time as an intern at the Salp\u00eatri\u00e8re hospital in Paris, where he observed [[Jean-Martin Charcot]]'s work with then so called \"hysterical\" patients.{{citation needed|date=April 2017}}\n\nIn 1887, Richet became professor of [[physiology]] at the [[Coll\u00e8ge de France]] investigating a variety of subjects such as [[neurochemistry]], [[digestion]], [[thermoregulation]] in [[Homeothermy|homeothermic]] animals, and [[breathing]].<ref name=\"Stewart 2012\">Wolf, Stewart. (2012). ''Brain, Mind, and Medicine: Charles Richet and the Origins of Physiological Psychology''. Transaction Publishers. pp. 1\u2013101. {{ISBN|1-56000-063-5}}</ref> In 1898, he became a member of the [[Acad\u00e9mie de M\u00e9decine]].{{citation needed|date=April 2017}} In 1914, he became a member of the [[French Academy of Sciences|Acad\u00e9mie des Sciences]].<ref name=\"Stewart 2012\"/>\n\nRichet discovered the analgesic drug [[chloralose]] with Maurice Hanriot.<ref name=\"Tabori 1972\">[[Paul Tabori|Tabori, Paul]]. (1972). ''Charles Richet''. In ''Pioneers of the Unseen''. Souvenir Press. pp. 98\u2013132. {{ISBN|0-285-62042-8}}</ref>\n\nRichet had many interests, and he wrote books about history, sociology, philosophy, psychology, as well as theatre and poetry. He was a pioneer in aviation.<ref name=\"Stewart 2012\"/>\n\nHe was involved in the French pacifist movement. Starting in 1902, pacifist societies began to meet at a National Peace Congress, often with several hundred attendees. Unable to unify the pacifist forces they set up a small permanent delegation of French Pacifist Societies in 1902, which Richet led, together with [[Lucien Le Foyer]] as secretary-general.<ref>{{cite book|chapter-url=http://jmguieu.free.fr/Enseignements/L3_France_Allemagne_Europe/TD_06_pacifisme.htm |last=Guieu |first=Jean-Michel|chapter=6 \u2013 Tensions nationalistes et efforts pacifistes|language=fr |year=2005|title=La France, l'Allemagne et l'Europe (1871\u20131945)|access-date=11 March 2015}}</ref>\n\n== Discovery of anaphylaxis ==\nRichet, working with Paul Portier, discovered the phenomenon of anaphylaxis.<ref name=\":1\">{{Cite journal |last=Richet |first=Gabriel |date=2003 |title=The discovery of anaphylaxis, a brief but triumphant encounter of two physiologists (1902) |url=https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14989211 |journal=Histoire des Sciences M\u00e9dicales |volume=37 |issue=4 |pages=463\u2013469 |pmid=14989211}}</ref> In 1901, they joined [[Albert I, Prince of Monaco]] on a scientific expedition around the French coast of Atlantic Ocean.<ref>{{Cite journal |last1=Dworetzky |first1=Murray |last2=Cohen |first2=Sheldon |last3=Cohen |first3=Sheldon G. |last4=Zelaya-Quesada |first4=Myrna |date=2002 |title=Portier, Richet, and the discovery of anaphylaxis: A centennial |url=https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S0091674902701188 |journal=Journal of Allergy and Clinical Immunology |language=en |volume=110 |issue=2 |pages=331\u2013336 |doi=10.1016/S0091-6749(02)70118-8|pmid=12170279 }}</ref> On board Albert's ship ''Princesse Alice II'', they extracted toxin (they called hypnotoxin) produced by cnidarians such as [[Portuguese man o' war]])<ref>{{Cite journal |last=Suput |first=Dusan |title=Interactions of Cnidarian Toxins with the Immune System |url=https://www.eurekaselect.com/article/33767 |journal=Inflammation & Allergy - Drug Targets |year=2011 |language=en |volume=10 |issue=5 |pages=429\u2013437 |doi=10.2174/187152811797200678|pmid=21824078 }}</ref> and sea anemone (''[[Actinia sulcata]]'').<ref name=\":2\">{{Cite journal |last1=Boden |first1=Stephen R. |last2=Wesley Burks |first2=A. |date=2011 |title=Anaphylaxis: a history with emphasis on food allergy: Anaphylaxis: a history with emphasis on food allergy |journal=Immunological Reviews |language=en |volume=242 |issue=1 |pages=247\u2013257 |doi=10.1111/j.1600-065X.2011.01028.x |pmc=3122150 |pmid=21682750}}</ref>\n\nIn their first experiment on the ship, they injected a dog with the toxin expecting the dog to develop immunity (tolerance to the toxin), which instead developed a severe immune reaction ([[hypersensitivity]]). In 1902, they repeated the injections in their laboratory and found that dogs normally tolerated the toxin at first injection, but subsequent injection after three weeks of the first, they always developed fatal shock, regardless of the dose on the toxin used.<ref name=\":2\" /> Thus, instead of inducing tolerance ([[prophylaxis]]) which they expected, they discovered effects of the toxin as deadly.<ref>{{Cite journal |last=May |first=Charles D. |date=1985 |title=The ancestry of allergy: Being an account of the original experimental induction of hypersensitivity recognizing the contribution of Paul Portier |url=https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S0091674985800221 |journal=Journal of Allergy and Clinical Immunology |language=en |volume=75 |issue=4 |pages=485\u2013495 |doi=10.1016/S0091-6749(85)80022-1|pmid=3884689 }}</ref>\n\nIn 1902, Richet coined the term ''aphylaxis'' to describe the phenomenon, but later changed it to ''anaphylaxis'' on grounds of [[euphony]].<ref name=\"His11\">{{cite journal |last=Boden |first=SR |author2=Wesley Burks, A |date=July 2011 |title=Anaphylaxis: a history with emphasis on food allergy |journal=Immunological Reviews |volume=242 |issue=1 |pages=247\u201357 |doi=10.1111/j.1600-065X.2011.01028.x |pmc=3122150 |pmid=21682750}}, citing May CD, \"The ancestry of allergy: being an account of the original experimental induction of hypersensitivity recognizing the contribution of Paul Portier\", ''J Allergy Clin Immunol.'' 1985 Apr; 75(4):485\u2013495.</ref> The term is from the [[Classical Greek|Greek]] \u1f00\u03bd\u03ac-, ''ana-'', meaning \"against\", and \u03c6\u03cd\u03bb\u03b1\u03be\u03b9\u03c2, ''phylaxis'', meaning \"protection\".<ref name=\"Dict\">{{cite web |title=anaphylaxis |url=http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/anaphylaxis |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100410192354/http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/anaphylaxis |archive-date=2010-04-10 |access-date=2009-11-21 |publisher=merriam-webster.com}}</ref> On 15 February 1902, Richet and Portier jointly presented their experiments before the Societ\u00e9 de Biologie in Paris.<ref>{{Cite web |title=De l'action anaphylactique de certains venins {{!}} Association des amis de la Biblioth\u00e8que nationale de France |url=http://sciences.amisbnf.org/fr/livre/de-laction-anaphylactique-de-certains-venins |access-date=2022-06-24 |website=sciences.amisbnf.org}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=De l'action anaphylactique de certains venins \u2013 ScienceOpen |url=https://www.scienceopen.com/document?vid=9be612fb-cbf5-45a0-b047-a1de36b6236c |access-date=2022-06-24 |website=www.scienceopen.com}}</ref> This is regarded as the birth of allergy (the term invented by [[Clemens von Pirquet]] in1906) study ([[allergology]]).<ref>{{Citation |last1=Ring |first1=Johannes |title=Anaphylaxis |date=2014 |url=https://www.karger.com/Article/FullText/358503 |journal=Chemical Immunology and Allergy |volume=100 |pages=54\u201361 |editor-last=Bergmann |editor-first=K.-C. |publisher=S. Karger AG |language=en |doi=10.1159/000358503 |isbn=978-3-318-02194-3 |access-date=2022-06-24 |last2=Grosber |first2=Martine |last3=Brockow |first3=Knut |last4=Bergmann |first4=Karl-Christian |pmid=24925384 |editor2-last=Ring |editor2-first=J.}}</ref> The research helped elucidate [[hay fever]], [[asthma]] and other [[Allergy|allergic reaction]]s to foreign substances and explained some previously not understood cases of [[Substance intoxication|intoxication]] and [[sudden cardiac death|sudden death]]. Richet continued to study on the phenomenon and was eventually awarded the [[Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine]] in 1913.<ref name=\":0\">{{Cite journal |last1=Androutsos |first1=G. |last2=Karamanou |first2=M. |last3=Stamboulis |first3=E. |last4=Liappas |first4=I. |last5=Lykouras |first5=E. |last6=Papadimitriou |first6=G. N. |date=2011 |title=The Nobel Prize laureate - father of anaphylaxis Charles-Robert Richet (1850-1935) and his anticancerous serum |url=https://jbuon.com/archive/16-4-783.pdf |journal=Journal of BUON |volume=16 |issue=4 |pages=783\u2013786 |pmid=22331744}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal |last1=Richet |first1=Gabriel |last2=Estingoy |first2=Pierrette |date=2003 |title=The life and times of Charles Richet |url=https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15025138 |journal=Histoire des Sciences M\u00e9dicales |volume=37 |issue=4 |pages=501\u2013513 |issn=0440-8888 |pmid=15025138}}</ref><ref name=\"nobel\" />\n\n==Parapsychology==\nRichet held a deep interest in [[extrasensory perception]] and hypnosis. In 1884, [[Alexandr Aksakov]] interested him in the [[Mediumship|medium]] of [[Eusapia Palladino]].{{citation needed|date=April 2017}} In 1891, Richet founded the ''Annales des sciences psychiques''. He kept in touch with renowned [[Occultism|occultists]] and [[Spiritualism|spiritualists]] of his time such as [[Albert von Schrenck-Notzing]], [[Frederic William Henry Myers]] and [[Gabriel Delanne]].{{citation needed|date=April 2017}} In 1919, Richet became honorary chairman of the [[Institut M\u00e9tapsychique International]] in Paris, and, in 1930, full-time president.<ref>[http://www.metapsychique.org/Charles-Richet.html \"Charles Richet\"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160311051340/http://www.metapsychique.org/Charles-Richet.html |date=11 March 2016 }}. Institut M\u00e9tapsychique International.</ref>\n\nAs a scientist, Richet was positive about a physical explanation for [[paranormal]] phenomena.<ref>{{cite journal |last=Alvarado |first=C. S. |year=2006 |title=Human radiations: Concepts of force in mesmerism, spiritualism and psychical research |journal=Journal of the Society for Psychical Research |volume=70 |pages=138\u2013162 |url=http://www.medicine.virginia.edu/clinical/departments/psychiatry/sections/cspp/dops/publicationslinks/Alvarado-Human-Radiations-JSPR-2006.pdf }}</ref> He wrote:  \"It has been shown that as regards subjective metapsychics the simplest and most rational explanation is to suppose the existence of a faculty of supernormal cognition ... setting in motion the human intelligence by certain vibrations that do not move the normal senses.\"<ref>{{cite book |last=Richet |first=C. |year=1923 |title=Thirty Years of Psychical Research |url=https://archive.org/details/b30010135 |location=New York |publisher=Macmillan |others=Translated from the second French edition }}</ref> In 1905, Richet was named president of the [[Society for Psychical Research]] in the United Kingdom.<ref>Berger, Arthur S; Berger, Joyce. (1995). ''Fear of the Unknown: Enlightened Aid-in-Dying''. Praeger. p. 35. {{ISBN|0-275-94683-5}} \"In 1905, Professor Charles Richet, the French physiologist on the faculty of medicine of Paris and winner of the Nobel Prize in 1913, was made its president. Although he was a materialist and positivist, he was drawn to psychical research.\"</ref>\n\n[[File:Linda Gazzera medium 2.png|thumb|[[Linda Gazzera]] a medium Richet investigated, in Paris, 1909.]]\n\nIn 1894, Richet coined the term [[ectoplasm (paranormal)|ectoplasm]].<ref>Blom, Jan Dirk. (2010). ''A Dictionary of Hallucinations''. Springer. p. 168. {{ISBN|978-1-4419-1222-0}}</ref> Richet believed that some mediumship could be explained physically due to the external projection of a material substance (ectoplasm) from the body of the medium, but denied this substance had anything to do with spirits. He rejected the [[Spiritualism|spirit hypothesis]] of mediumship as unscientific, instead supporting the sixth sense hypothesis.<ref name=\"Tabori 1972\"/><ref>Ashby, Robert H. (1972). ''The Guidebook for the Study of Psychical Research''. Rider. pp. 162\u2013179</ref> According to Richet:\n\n<blockquote>It seems to me prudent not to give credence to the spiritistic hypothesis... it appears to me still (at the present time, at all events) improbable, for it contradicts (at least apparently) the most precise and definite data of physiology, whereas the hypothesis of the sixth sense is a new physiological notion which contradicts nothing that we learn from physiology. Consequently, although in certain rare cases spiritism supplies an apparently simpler explanation, I cannot bring myself to accept it. When we have fathomed the history of these unknown vibrations emanating from reality \u2013 past reality, present reality, and even future reality \u2013 we shall doubtless have given them an unwonted degree of importance. The history of the Hertzian waves shows us the ubiquity of these vibrations in the external world, imperceptible to our senses.<ref name=\"Richet 1928\"/></blockquote>\n\nHe hypothesized a \"sixth sense\", an ability to perceive hypothetical vibrations, which he discussed in his 1928 book ''Our Sixth Sense''.<ref name=\"Richet 1928\">Richet, Charles. (nd, ca 1928). ''Our Sixth Sense''. London: Rider. (First published in French, 1928)</ref> Although he believed in extrasensory perception, Richet did not believe in [[life after death]] or spirits.<ref name=\"Tabori 1972\"/>\n\nHe investigated and studied various mediums such as [[Eva Carri\u00e8re]], [[William Eglinton]], [[Pascal Forthuny]], [[Stefan Ossowiecki]], [[Leonora Piper]] and [[Raphael Schermann]].<ref name=\"Tabori 1972\"/> From 1905\u20131910, Richet attended many s\u00e9ances with the medium [[Linda Gazzera]] claiming she was a genuine medium who had performed [[psychokinesis]], i.e. that various objects had been moved in the s\u00e9ance room.<ref name=\"Tabori 1972\"/> Gazzera was exposed as a fraud in 1911.<ref>[[Joseph McCabe|McCabe, Joseph]].
