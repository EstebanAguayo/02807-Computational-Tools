|website=crcc.usc.edu |language=en-US}}</ref>\n\n==Career==\nAfter completion of his studies in [[France]] (1989), Mukwege returned to work at the Lemera Hospital. At the beginning of the [[First Congo War]], the Lemera hospital was attacked, his patients and co-workers were killed and the hospital ransacked. Dr. Mukwege fled to Bukavu where he founded the Panzi Hospital in 1999.<ref name=\":1\" /> Its construction was mainly financed by Swedish Christian aid organizations and the [[Swedish International Development Cooperation Agency]]. The Panzi Hospital has continued to enjoy support from the Swedish Pentecostal Mission's development cooperation organization PMU.<ref name=\"PCPJ\">{{Cite web |url= https://pcpj.org/2018/10/07/pentecostal-nobel-peace-prize-winner-denis-mukwege-gives-glory-to-god/ |title= Pentecostal Nobel Peace Prize Winner Denis Mukwege Gives Glory to God |date= 7 October 2018 | publisher= PCPJ |language=en|access-date=8 December 2018}}</ref>\n\nSince its foundation, [[Panzi Hospital]] has treated more than 82,000 patients with complex gynecological damage and trauma.<ref>{{Cite web |title=How Dr. Denis Mukwege Became a Global Advocate for Rape Survivors |url=https://deeply.thenewhumanitarian.org/womenandgirls/articles/2017/10/12/how-a-drc-surgeon-became-a-global-advocate-for-rape-survivors |access-date=2022-04-26 |website=Women and Girls |language=en}}</ref> An estimated 60 percent of these injuries result from instances of sexual violence being used as a weapon of war, with most of the patients of the time coming from conflict zones. Mukwege has described how his patients arrived at the hospital sometimes naked, usually in horrific condition.<ref name= \"Nolen\" /> When he observed that different armed groups were using genital damaging as a [[Wartime sexual violence|weapon of war]] during the conflict of the late 1990s, Mukwege devoted himself to [[reconstructive surgery]] to help female victims of sexual violence.<ref name= \":1\" /> The German Institute for Medical Mission (DIFAEM) has been supporting Mukwege's work with funds and medicines.<ref>{{Citation | language = de | url = http://pressemitteilungen.epo.de/?p=948 | archive-url = https://archive.today/20130221145609/http://pressemitteilungen.epo.de/?p=948 | url-status = dead | title = Dif\u00e4m: Projektpartner Dr. Denis Mukwege erh\u00e4lt den Menschenrechtspreis der Vereinten Nationen. In 2016 the Mukwege Foundation was created, to promote the aims of the Panzi Hospital and Foundations worldwide and \"advocate for an end to wartime sexual violence everywhere.\"<ref The experience was so overwhelming that I knew my life was forever changed by that moment.\"<ref>{{cite book |last1=\u00c5kerlund |first1=Berthil |year=2018 |title=Denis Mukwege: en levnadsber\u00e4ttelse |trans-title= Denis Mukwege: a life story |publisher=Weyler |page=97 |isbn=9789185849826 }}</ref> Apart from working as a doctor, he partly ministers in a Pentecostal church in Bukavu with 700 members.<ref>[https://www.franceinter.fr/monde/nobel-de-la-paix-le-medecin-congolais-denis-mukwege-recompense Nobel de la paix : le m\u00e9decin congolais Denis Mukwege r\u00e9compens\u00e9] (in French)</ref> Mukwege has repeatedly named his faith in Jesus Christ as a primary [[motivation]] for his work at Panzi.<ref>{{cite web|title=Congolese Pentecostals see Denis Mukwege, Nobel Prize-winning doctor, as a 'blessing'|url=https://religionnews.com/2018/10/18/congolese-pentecostals-see-denis-mukwege-nobel-prize-winning-doctor-as-a-blessing/|work=Religion News Service|date=18 October 2018|access-date=8 December 2018|language=en-US}}</ref>\n\nThe Panzi Hospital is being run by the Congolese Pentecostal movement CEPAC and has been continuously supported by the Swedish Pentecostal movement.
