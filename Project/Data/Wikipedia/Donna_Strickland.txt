She was awarded the [[Nobel Prize in Physics]] in 2018, together with [[G\u00e9rard Mourou]], for the practical implementation of [[chirped pulse amplification]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/science/2018/oct/02/arthur-ashkin-gerard-mourou-and-donna-strickland-win-nobel-physics-prize|title=Physics Nobel prize won by Arthur Ashkin, G\u00e9rard Mourou and Donna Strickland|date=2 October 2018|work=[[The Guardian]]|access-date=4 October 2018|last1=Sample|first1=Ian|last2=Davis|first2=Nicola}}</ref> She is a [[professor]] at the [[University of Waterloo]] in [[Ontario]], Canada.<ref name=\"UW\">{{cite web|url=https://uwaterloo.ca/physics-astronomy/people-profiles/donna-strickland|title=Donna Strickland|date=2 October 2018|publisher=[[University of Waterloo]]|access-date=2 October 2018}}</ref>\n\nShe served as fellow, vice president, and president of [[Optica (society)|Optica (formerly OSA)]], and is currently chair of its Presidential Advisory Committee. In 2018, she was listed as one of [[100 Women (BBC)|BBC's 100 Women]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-46225037|title=BBC 100 Women 2018: Who is on the list?|date=19 November 2018|work=BBC News|access-date=23 July 2019|language=en-GB}}</ref>\n\n==Early life and education==\n\nStrickland was born on 27 May 1959, in [[Guelph]], Ontario, Canada to Edith J. ({{n\u00e9e|Ranney}}), an English teacher,<ref name=whirlwind>{{cite news |last1=Booth|first1=Laura |title=Scientist caught in a Nobel whirlwind |url=https://www.therecord.com/news-story/8943888-scientist-caught-in-a-nobel-whirlwind/?s=n1 |access-date=4 October 2018 |work=[[Waterloo Region Record]] |date=3 October 2018}}</ref> and Lloyd Strickland, an electrical engineer.<ref name=\"Thesis\" /> After graduating from Guelph Collegiate Vocational Institute, she decided to attend [[McMaster University]] because its [[engineering physics]] program included lasers and [[electro-optics]], areas of particular interest to her.<ref name=\"whirlwind\" /> At McMaster, she was one of three women in a class of twenty-five. Strickland graduated with a [[Bachelor of Engineering]] degree in engineering physics in 1981.<ref name=\"globeandmail\">{{cite news |last1=Semeniuk |first1=Ivan |title=Canada's newest Nobel Prize winner, Donna Strickland, 'just wanted to do something fun' |url=https://www.theglobeandmail.com/world/article-canadian-scientist-donna-strickland-shares-nobel-physics-prize/ |access-date=3 October 2018 |work=[[The Globe and Mail]] |date=2 October 2018}}</ref>\n\nStrickland studied for her graduate degree in [[The Institute of Optics]],<ref name=\"jewel\">{{cite book |last=Mourou |first=G\u00e9rard  |author-link=G\u00e9rard_Mourou |editor1-last=Stroud |editor1-first=Carlos |title=A Jewel in the Crown: 75th Anniversary Essays of The Institute of Optics of the University of Rochester |date=2004 |publisher=Meliora Press |location=Rochester, NY |page=272 |chapter-url=http://www2.optics.rochester.edu/~stroud/BookHTML/ChapVI_pdf/VI_53.pdf |url=http://www2.optics.rochester.edu/~stroud/BookHTML/contents.htm |chapter=53. news|url=http://www.rochester.edu/newscenter/rochesters-breakthrough-in-laser-science-earns-nobel-prize-340302/|title=Rochester breakthrough in laser science earns Nobel Prize|last=Valich|first=Lindsey|date=2 October 2018|work=Newscenter|access-date=4 October 2018|publisher=[[University of Rochester]]}}</ref>\nStrickland and Mourou worked to develop an experimental setup that could raise the peak power of laser pulses, to overcome a limitation, that when the maximal intensity of laser pulses reached gigawatts per square centimetre, [[self-focusing]] of the pulses severely damaged the amplifying part of the laser.\nTheir 1985 technique of [[chirped pulse amplification]] stretched out each laser pulse both spectrally and in time before amplifying it, then compressed each pulse back to its original duration, generating ultrashort optical pulses of [[terawatt]] to [[petawatt]] intensity.<ref name=\"Thesis\" />\nUsing chirped pulse amplification allowed smaller high-power laser systems to be built on a typical laboratory [[optical table]], as \"table-top terawatt lasers\".<ref name=\"Valich Rochester\" />\nThe work received the 2018 [[Nobel Prize in Physics]].<ref name=\"bbc2\">{{cite news |last1=Murphy |first1=Jessica |title=Donna Strickland: The 'laser jock' Nobel prize winner |url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-us-canada-45722890 |access-date=2 October 2018 |work=[[BBC News]] |date=2 October 2018}}</ref>\n\n==Career and research==\n[[File:Ultrafast Laser group.jpg|thumb|right|upright=1.5|Strickland's [[ultrafast laser]] group at the [[University of Waterloo]] in 2017]]\n\nFrom 1988 to 1991, Strickland was a research associate at the [[National Research Council of Canada]], where she worked with [[Paul Corkum]] in the Ultrafast Phenomena Section, which had the distinction at that time of having produced the most powerful short-pulse laser in the world.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/24232914/laser_lab_makes_short_work_of_super_beam/|title=Laser lab makes short work of super beam|last=Page|first=Shelley|date=19 October 1990|work=[[Ottawa Citizen]]}}</ref> She worked in the laser division of [[Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory]] from 1991 to 1992 and joined the technical staff of [[Princeton University]]'s Advanced Technology Center for Photonics and Opto-electronic Materials in 1992. She joined the [[University of Waterloo]] in 1997 as an [[assistant professor]].<ref name=\"TOS\" /> She became the first full-time female professor in physics at the University of Waterloo.<ref name=\"Nusca\">{{cite news |last=Nusca |first=Andrew |url=http://fortune.com/2018/10/16/nobel-donna-strickland-women-physics/ |title=Nobel Laureate Donna Strickland: Yes, Women Are Joining Physics. But We've Got Work to Do |work=[[Fortune (magazine)|Fortune]] |date=17 October 2018 |access-date=17 October 2018 }}</ref> Strickland is currently a professor, leading an [[ultrafast laser]] group that develops high-intensity laser systems for nonlinear optics investigations.<ref name=\"UW\" /> She has described herself as a \"laser jock\":<ref name=\"bbc2\" />\n{{quote|I think it's because we thought we were good with our hands. As an experimentalist,<!-- punctuation corrected --> you need to understand the physics,<!-- punctuation corrected --> but you also need to be able to actually make something work, and the lasers were very finicky in those days.<ref name=\"whirlwind\" />}}\n\nStrickland's recent work has focused on pushing the boundaries of ultrafast optical science to new [[wavelength]] ranges such as the [[mid-infrared]] and the [[ultraviolet]], using techniques such as two-colour or multi-frequency methods, as well as [[Raman scattering|Raman generation]].<ref name=\"UW\" /> She is also working on the role of high-power lasers in the microcrystalline lens of the human eye, during the process of micromachining of the eye lens to cure [[presbyopia]].<ref name=\"UW\" />\n\nStrickland became a fellow of Optica{{efn|Then known as Optical Society of America (OSA).}} in 2008. She served as its vice president and president in 2011 and 2013 respectively, and was a topical editor of its journal ''[[Optics Letters]]'' from 2004 to 2010.<ref name=\"UW\" /><ref name=\"TOS2\">{{cite web|url=https://www.osa.org/en-us/about_osa/newsroom/news_releases/2018/arthur_ashkin_gerard_mourou_and_donna_strickland_a/|title=Arthur Ashkin, G\u00e9rard Mourou, and Donna [[Arthur Ashkin]] received the other half of the prize for unrelated work on [[optical tweezers]]. She became the third woman ever to be awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics, after [[Marie Curie]] in 1903 and [[Maria Goeppert Mayer]] in 1963.<ref name=\"UW\" /><ref>{{Cite news |url=https://www.bbc.com/news/science-environment-45655151 |title=First woman Physics Nobel winner in 55 years |work=[[BBC News]] |first=Paul |last=Rincon |date=2 October 2018 |access-date=2 October 2018}}</ref>\n\nStrickland and Mourou published their pioneering work \"Compression of amplified chirped optical pulses\" in 1985, while Strickland was still a doctoral student under Mourou.{{efn|Strickland attempted to add Steve Williamson as an author of the article, but Williamson removed the name as \"he hadn't done enough\".<ref name=\"whirlwind\" /><ref>{{Cite journal|date=15 October 1985 |title=Compression of amplified chirped optical pulses |journal=[[Optics Communications]] |volume=55 |issue=6 |pages=447\u2013449 |doi=10.1016/0030-4018(85)90151-8 |issn=0030-4018 |last1=Strickland |first1=Donna |last2=Mourou |first2=Gerard|citeseerx=10.1.1.673.148 |bibcode=1985OptCo..55..447S }}</ref>}} Their invention of chirped pulse amplification for lasers at the Laboratory for Laser Energetics in Rochester<ref name=\"Valich Rochester\" /> led to the development of the field of high-intensity ultrashort pulses of light beams. Because the ultrabrief and ultrasharp light beams are capable of making extremely precise cuts, the technique is used in [[Laser machining|laser micromachining]], [[laser surgery]], medicine, [[fundamental science]] studies, and other applications. It has enabled doctors to perform millions of corrective [[laser eye surgery|laser eye surgeries]].<ref>{{cite web |title='Optical Tweezers' and Tools Used for Laser Eye Surgery Snag Physics Nobel |url=https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/optical-tweezers-and-tools-used-for-laser-eye-surgery-snag-physics-nobel1/ |access-date=2 October 2018 |work=[[Scientific American]] |date=2 October 2018}}</ref> She said that after developing the technique they knew it would be a significant discovery.<ref name=\"bbc2\" />\n\nWhen she received the Nobel Prize, many commentators were surprised that she had not reached the rank of full professor. In response, Strickland said that she had \"never applied\" for a professorship;<ref name=\"CHE \u2018I Never Applied\u2019\">{{Cite journal |last1=Crowe |first1=Cailin |title='I Never Applied': Nobel
