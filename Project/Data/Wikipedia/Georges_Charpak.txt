Charpak's family moved from [[Poland]] to [[Paris]] when he was seven years old, beginning his study of mathematics in 1941 at the Lyc\u00e9e Saint Louis.<ref name=\"Scientific Information Service \">CERN {{cite book| url = http://library.web.cern.ch/library/Archives/isad/isacharpak.html| title = Scientific Information Service - Archive| publisher = CERN| access-date = 2012-01-29| archive-date = 2012-02-14| archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20120214055231/http://library.web.cern.ch/library/Archives/isad/isacharpak.html| url-status = dead}}</ref> The actor and film director [[Andr\u00e9 Charpak]] was his younger brother.\n\nDuring [[World War II]] Charpak served in the [[French Resistance|resistance]] and was imprisoned by [[Vichy France|Vichy]] authorities in 1943. In 1944 he was deported to the [[Nazis|Nazi]] [[concentration camp]] at [[Dachau concentration camp|Dachau]], where he remained until the camp was liberated in 1945.\n\nAfter ''[[Classe pr\u00e9paratoire aux grandes \u00e9coles|classes pr\u00e9paratoires]]'' studies at [[Lyc\u00e9e Saint-Louis]] in Paris and later at [[Citadel of Montpellier|Lyc\u00e9e Joffre]] in Montpellier,<ref>[http://www.lyceejoffre.net/cpge/blog/2010/10/15/georges-charpak-1924-2010/#more-899 \"Tribulations d\u2019un immigr\u00e9 d\u2019Europe centrale, Georges Charpak\"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120324223102/http://www.lyceejoffre.net/cpge/blog/2010/10/15/georges-charpak-1924-2010/#more-899 |date=2012-03-24 }} on Lyc\u00e9e Joffre website {{in lang|fr}}</ref> he joined in 1945 the Paris-based [[\u00c9cole nationale sup\u00e9rieure des mines de Paris|\u00c9cole des Mines]], one of the most prestigious engineering schools in France. The following year he became a naturalized French citizen. He received his PhD in 1954<ref>[https://www.osti.gov/biblio/4395224 Charpak, G.; Suzor, F. - 1954 May 01]   Retrieved 2012-01-29</ref> in [[nuclear physics]] at the Coll\u00e8ge de France, receiving the qualification after having written a thesis on the subject of very-low-energy radiation due to disintegration of nuclei (Charpak & Suzor).<ref name=\"Scientific Information Service \"/><ref>See : [[Isotopes of actinium|<sup>228</sup>Ac = (Actinium 228)]]</ref><ref>F.Suzor et G.Charpak 1959 (June) ''Study of the electrons and X-rays of autoionisation with Beta radiation of [[Isotopes of promethium#Promethium-147|Prometheum 147]]'' - [http://hal.archives-ouvertes.fr/docs/00/23/61/12/PDF/ajp-jphysrad_1959_20_6_647_0.pdf archive]</ref>\n\nIn 1959,  he joined the staff of [[CERN]] ([[CERN|European Organization for Nuclear Research]]) in [[Geneva]], where he invented and developed<ref>{{Cite journal |doi = 10.1016/0029-554X(70)90872-4|title = Investigation of some properties of multiwire proportional chambers|journal = Nuclear Instruments and Methods|volume = 88|issue = 1|pages = 149\u2013161|year = 1970|last1 = Bouclier|first1 = R.|last2 = Charpak|first2 = G.|last3 = Dim\u010dovski|first3 = Z.|last4 = Fischer|first4 = G.|last5 = Sauli|first5 = F.|last6 = Coignet|first6 = G.|last7 = Fl\u00fcgge|first7 = G.|bibcode = 1970NucIM..88..149B}}</ref> the [[multiwire proportional chamber]]. The chamber was patented and that quickly superseded the old bubble chambers, allowing for better data processing.<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Giomataris|first1=Ioannis|title=Georges Charpak-a true man of science|journal=CERN Courier|date=December 2010|volume=50|issue=10|pages=33\u201336|url=https://cds.cern.ch/record/1734475}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|last1=Catapano|first1=Paola|title=Georges Charpak: hardwired for science|journal=CERN Courier|date=March 2009|volume=42|issue=2|pages=24\u201328|url=https://cds.cern.ch/record/1734347}}</ref> This new creation had been made public during 1968.<ref>[[IEEE]][http://www.ieeeghn.org/wiki/index.php/Milestones:CERN_Experimental_Instrumentation,_1968 ieeeghn] Retrieved 2012-01-29</ref>   Charpak was later to become a joint inventor with Nlolc and Policarpo of the scintillation drift chamber during the latter parts of the 1970s.<ref>Elena Aprile, Aleksey E. In 1980, Georges Charpak became professor-in-residence at \u00c9cole sup\u00e9rieure de physique et de chimie industrielles in Paris ([[ESPCI]]) and held the Joliot-Curie Chair there in 1984.  This is where he developed and demonstrated the powerful applications of the particle detectors he invented, most notably for enabling better health diagnostics. \nHe was the co-founder of a number of start-up in the biolab arena, including Molecular Engines Laboratories, Biospace Instruments and SuperSonic Imagine \u2013 together with [[Mathias Fink]]. He was elected to the [[French Academy of Sciences]] on 20 May 1985.\n\nGeorges Charpak was awarded the [[Nobel Prize in Physics]] in 1992 \"for his invention and development of [[particle detector]]s, in particular the [[multiwire proportional chamber]]\", with affiliations to both \u00c9cole sup\u00e9rieure de physique et de chimie industrielles ([[ESPCI]]) and CERN. This was the last time a single person was awarded the physics prize, as of 2020. In 1999, Charpak received the Golden Plate Award of the [[Academy of Achievement|American Academy of Achievement]].<ref>{{cite web|title= Golden Plate Awardees of the American Academy of Achievement |website=www.achievement.org|publisher=[[American Academy of Achievement]]|url=https://achievement.org/our-history/golden-plate-awards/#science-exploration}}</ref>\n\nIn France, Charpak was a very strong [[pro-nuclear movement|advocate for nuclear power]].
