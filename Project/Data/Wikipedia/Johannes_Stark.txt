This phenomenon is known as the [[Stark effect]].\n\nStark received his Ph.D. in physics from the [[University of Munich]] in 1897 under the supervision of [[Eugen von Lommel]], and served as Lommel's assistant until his appointment as a lecturer at the [[University of G\u00f6ttingen]] in 1900. He was an [[wikt:extraordinary professor|extraordinary professor]] at [[Leibniz University Hannover]] from 1906 until he became a professor at [[RWTH Aachen University]] in 1909. In 1917, he became professor at the [[University of Greifswald]], and he also worked at the [[University of W\u00fcrzburg]] from 1920 to 1922.\n\nA supporter of [[Adolf Hitler]] from 1924, Stark was one of the main figures, along with fellow Nobel laureate [[Philipp Lenard]], in the anti-Semitic ''[[Deutsche Physik]]'' movement, which sought to remove Jewish scientists from German physics. He was appointed head of the [[Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft|German Research Foundation]] in 1933 and was president of the [[Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt|Reich Physical-Technical Institute]] from 1933 to 1939. In 1947 he was found guilty as a \"Major Offender\" by a [[denazification]] court.\n\n==Biography==\n\n===Early years===\nBorn in [[Freihung|Schickenhof]], [[Kingdom of Bavaria]] (now [[Freihung]]), Stark was educated at  [[Bayreuth Gymnasium]] (secondary school) and later in [[Regensburg]]. His [[college|collegiate]] education began at the [[University of Munich]], where he studied physics, [[mathematics]], [[chemistry]], and [[crystallography]]. His tenure at that college began in 1894; he graduated in 1897, with his [[doctorate|doctoral]] dissertation titled ''Untersuchung \u00fcber einige physikalische, vorz\u00fcglich optische Eigenschaften des Ru\u00dfes '' (Investigation of some physical, in particular optical properties of soot).<ref>[http://opacplus.bsb-muenchen.de/search?oclcno=162968418 Entry in the catalogue of the Bavarian State Library, MunIch]. Opacplus.bsb-muenchen.de. Retrieved on 2012-07-27.</ref>\n\n===Career===\nStark worked in various positions at the Physics Institute of his [[alma mater]] until 1900, when he became an unsalaried [[lecture]]r at the [[University of G\u00f6ttingen]]. An extraordinary professor at [[Leibniz University Hannover|Hannover]] by 1906, in 1908 he became professor at [[RWTH Aachen University]]. He worked and researched at physics departments of several universities, including the [[University of Greifswald]], until 1922. In 1919, he won the [[Nobel Prize in Physics]] for his \"discovery of the [[Doppler effect]] in [[canal rays]] and the splitting of spectral lines in electric fields\" (the latter is known as the [[Stark effect]]). From 1933 until his retirement in 1939, Stark was elected President of the [[Physikalisch-Technische Reichsanstalt]], while also President of the [[Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft]].\n\nIt was Stark who, as the editor of the ''Jahrbuch der Radioaktivit\u00e4t und Elektronik'', asked in 1907, then still rather unknown, [[Albert Einstein]] to write a review article on the [[principle of relativity]]. Stark seemed impressed by relativity and Einstein's earlier work when he quoted \"the principle of relativity formulated by H. A. Lorentz and A. Einstein\" and \"Planck's relationship ''M''<sub>0</sub> = ''E''<sub>0</sub>/''c''<sup>2</sup>\" in his 1907 paper<ref>{{cite journal|author=Stark, J.|url=https://archive.org/details/physikalischeze00unkngoog|title=Elementarquantum der Energie, Modell der negativen und der positiven Elekrizitat|journal=Physikalische Zeitschrift|page=881|volume=24|issue= 8 |date=1907}}</ref> in [[Physikalische Zeitschrift]], where he used the equation ''e''<sub>0</sub> = ''m''<sub>0</sub>''c''<sup>2</sup> to calculate an \"elementary quantum of energy\", i.e. the amount of energy related to the mass of an electron at rest. While working on his article,<ref>{{cite journal|author=Einstein, A.|title=Ueber das Relativitatprinzip und die aus demselb gezogenen Folgerungen|journal=Jahrbuch der Radioaktivit\u00e4t und Elektronik|page=411|volume= IV |date=1907}} Translated in Schwartz, H. M. (1977) [http://physics.uark.edu/publications/volume1/h.m.schwartz.pdf Einstein's comprehensive 1907 essay on relativity, parts I, II, III] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130315094043/http://physics.uark.edu/publications/volume1/H.M.SCHWARTZ.pdf |date=2013-03-15 }}, ''American Journal of Physics'', June, September and October, 1977.</ref> Einstein began a line of thought that would eventually lead to his [[general relativity|generalized theory of relativity]], which in turn became (after its confirmation) the start of Einstein's worldwide fame. This is ironic, given Stark's later work as an anti-Einstein and [[Criticism of relativity theory|anti-relativity]] propagandist in the ''Deutsche Physik'' movement.<ref>Norton, John D. \"Einstein and Nordstr\u00f6m: Some Lesser-Known Thought Experiments in Gravitation\", [[John Earman]], Michel Janssen, John D. Norton (eds.), [[Birkh\u00e4user]], 1993, pp. 3\u201329 (p. 6). {{ISBN|0-8176-3624-2}}</ref>\n\nStark published more than 300 papers, mainly regarding [[electricity]] and other such topics. He received various awards, including the [[Nobel Prize]], the Baumgartner Prize of the [[Vienna Academy of Sciences]] (1910), the Vahlbruch Prize of the [[G\u00f6ttingen Academy of Sciences]] (1914), and the [[Matteucci Medal]] of the [[Accademia nazionale delle scienze detta dei XL|Rome Academy]]. Probably his best known contribution to the field of [[physics]] is the [[Stark effect]], which he discovered in 1913.  In 1970 the [[International Astronomical Union]] honored him with a [[Stark (crater)|crater]] on the far-side of the moon, without knowing about his Nazi activities.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.prospectmagazine.co.uk/science-and-technology/astronomists-unknowingly-dedicated-moon-craters-to-nazis-will-the-next-historical-reckoning-be-at-cosmic-level|title=Astronomers unknowingly dedicated moon craters to Nazis|date=26 June 2020|author=Philipp Ball|website=prospectmagazine.co.uk|access-date=27 June 2020}}</ref> The name was dropped on August 12, 2020.<ref>[https://planetarynames.wr.usgs.gov/Feature/5683 Gazetteer of Planetary Nomenclature]</ref>\n\nHe married [[Luise Uepler]], and they had five children.  His hobbies were the cultivation of fruit trees and forestry. He worked in his private laboratory, which he set up using his Nobel prize money, on his country estate in Upper Bavaria after the second world war.  There he studied the deflection of light in an electric field.<ref>[http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/physics/laureates/1919/stark-bio.html Johannes Stark \u2013 Biography]. Nobelprize.org. Retrieved on 2012-07-27.</ref>\n\n===Affiliation with Nazism===\n<!-- Deleted image removed: [[Image:AdolfHitlerMaalOgPersonlighed.jpg|thumb|right|The cover of a 1932 Danish translation of Stark's \"Adolf Hitler: Aims and Personality\"]] -->\n\nFrom 1924 onwards, Stark supported Hitler.<ref name=ball>{{cite book |first=Philip |last=Ball |title=Serving the Reich: The Struggle for the Soul of Physics under Hitler |url=https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/how-2-pro-nazi-nobelists-attacked-einstein-s-jewish-science-excerpt1/|publisher=The University of Chicago Press |year=2014}}</ref> During the [[Nazism|Nazi]] regime, Stark attempted to become the [[F\u00fchrer]] of German physics through the ''[[Deutsche Physik]]'' (\"German physics\") movement (along with fellow Nobel laureate [[Philipp Lenard]]) against the \"Jewish physics\" of [[Albert Einstein]] and [[Werner Heisenberg]] (who was not Jewish). After Werner Heisenberg defended Albert Einstein's [[theory of relativity]], Stark wrote an angry article in the official [[Schutzstaffel|SS]] newspaper ''[[Das Schwarze Korps]]'', calling Heisenberg a \"White Jew\".<ref name=ball/>\n\nOn August 21, 1934, Stark wrote to physicist and fellow Nobel laureate [[Max von Laue]], telling him to toe the party line or suffer the consequences. The letter was signed off with \"Heil Hitler.\"<ref>Reisman, Arnold (2006) [http://www.newacademia.com/turkeys_modernization/ ''Turkey's Modernization: Refugees from Nazism and Atat\u00fcrk's Vision''] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070103054642/http://www.newacademia.com/turkeys_modernization/ |date=2007-01-03 }}. {{ISBN|978-0-9777908-8-3}}<!-- Note: While the book title seems to be about Turkey, the index does have references to von Laue and Stark, which is the best I can do for confirming the reference. --></ref>\n\nIn his 1934 book ''[[Nationalsozialismus und Wissenschaft]]'' (English: \"National Socialism and Science\") Stark maintained that the priority of the scientist was to serve the nation\u2014thus, the important fields of research were those that could help German arms production and industry.  He attacked theoretical physics as \"Jewish\" and stressed that scientific positions in [[Nazi Germany]] should only be held by pure-blooded Germans.\n\nWriting in ''Das Schwarze Korps'', Stark argued that even if [[racial antisemitism]] were to triumph, it would only be a 'partial victory' if 'Jewish' ideas were not similarly defeated: \"We also have to eradicate the Jewish spirit, whose blood can flow just as undisturbed today as before if its carriers hold beautiful [[Aryan]] passes\".<ref>Stone, Dan \"Nazi Race Ideologues\", in ''Patterns of Prejudice'', Volume 50, Issue 4-5 (2016), p. 452.</ref>\n\nIn 1947, following the defeat of Germany in [[World War II]], Stark was classified as a \"Major Offender\" and received a sentence of four years' imprisonment (later suspended) by a [[denazification]] court.\n\n===Later life and death===\n\nStark spent the last years of his life on his Gut Eppenstatt near Traunstein in Upper Bavaria, where he died in 1957 at the age of 83.
