{{post-nominals|post-noms=[[Order of St. Gregory the Great|KCSG]]}} (18 January 1937{{spnd}}3 August 2020) was an [[Irish nationalism|Irish nationalist]]<!--Please discuss any change on talkpage--> politician from [[Northern Ireland]], widely regarded as one of the most important figures in the recent political history of Ireland, as one of the architects of the [[Northern Ireland peace process]].\n\nA native of [[Derry]], he was a founding member of the [[Social Democratic and Labour Party]] (SDLP), and served as its second leader from 1979 to 2001. He also served as a [[Member of the European Parliament]] (MEP), and a [[Member of Parliament (United Kingdom)|Member of the UK Parliament]] (MP), as well as a member of the [[Northern Ireland Assembly]] (MLA).\n\nHume was co-recipient of the 1998 [[Nobel Peace Prize]] with [[David Trimble]], and also received both the [[Gandhi Peace Prize]] and the [[King Center for Nonviolent Social Change|Martin Luther King Award]].  He is the only person to receive the three major peace awards.\n\nIn 2012, Pope [[Benedict XVI]] made Hume a Knight Commander of the Papal [[Order of St. Gregory the Great]].<ref>{{cite news | url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-northern-ireland-foyle-west-18733621 | work=BBC News | title=John Hume knighted by Pope Benedict | date=6 July 2012 | access-date=20 June 2018 | archive-date=21 October 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20181021143154/https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-northern-ireland-foyle-west-18733621 | url-status=live }}</ref>\nHe was named \"[[Ireland's Greatest]]\" in a 2010 public poll by Irish national broadcaster [[RT\u00c9]] to find the greatest person in Ireland's history.<ref>{{cite news | url=http://www.rte.ie/news/2010/1023/irelandsgreatest.html | work=RT\u00c9 News | title=John Hume proud of 'Ireland's Greatest' award | date=26 October 2010 | access-date=17 January 2011 | archive-date=2 November 2012 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121102181234/http://www.rte.ie/news/2010/1023/irelandsgreatest.html | url-status=live }}</ref>\n\n==Early life and education==\n\nHume was born in 1937 into a working-class Catholic family in Derry, the eldest of seven children of Anne \"Annie\" (n\u00e9e Doherty), a seamstress, and Samuel Hume, a shipyard worker.<ref>{{Cite book|url = https://books.google.com/books?id=92bJlWbPeKMC&q=John+Hume+Anne+Doherty|title = Who's who in the World|isbn = 9780837911106|author1 = Marquis|year = 1990|access-date = 19 September 2020|archive-date = 23 September 2021|archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20210923144853/https://books.google.com/books?id=92bJlWbPeKMC&q=John+Hume+Anne+Doherty|url-status = live}}</ref> He had a mostly [[Irish Catholics|Irish Catholic]] background; though his surname derived from one of his great-grandfathers, a [[Scottish people|Scottish]] [[Presbyterianism#Scotland|Presbyterian]] who migrated to [[County Donegal]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Ceasefire: It's all just coming together for the fixer: John Hume risked all when he met Sinn Fein. Now there's talk of a Nobel Peace Prize. Cal McCrystal reports|first=Cal|last=McCrystal|date=4 September 1994|url=https://www.independent.co.uk/voices/ceasefire-its-all-just-coming-together-for-the-fixer-john-hume-risked-all-when-he-met-sinn-fein-now-theres-talk-of-a-nobel-peace-prize-cal-mccrystal-reports-1446738.html|access-date=3 November 2017|archive-date=20 February 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150220175724/http://www.independent.co.uk/voices/ceasefire-its-all-just-coming-together-for-the-fixer-john-hume-risked-all-when-he-met-sinn-fein-now-theres-talk-of-a-nobel-peace-prize-cal-mccrystal-reports-1446738.html|url-status=live}}</ref> Hume attended [[St Columb's College]] and went on to [[St Patrick's College, Maynooth]], the leading Catholic seminary in Ireland and a recognised college of the [[National University of Ireland]], where he intended to study for the priesthood. Among his teachers was [[Tom\u00e1s \u00d3 Fiaich]], the future cardinal and [[Primate of All Ireland]].<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ta2CAAAAIAAJ&q=%22john+hume%22+teacher+%22%C3%93+Fiaich%22|title=John Hume, Statesman of the Troubles|publisher=Blackstaff Press|year=1984|last=White|first=Barry|page=29|isbn=9780856403279|access-date=19 September 2020|archive-date=23 September 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210923144852/https://books.google.com/books?id=ta2CAAAAIAAJ&q=%22john+hume%22+teacher+%22%C3%93+Fiaich%22|url-status=live}}</ref>\n\nHume did not complete his clerical studies but did obtain an M.A. degree in French and history from the college in 1958, and then returned home to his native Derry, where he became a teacher at his Alma mater, St Columb's College. He was a founding member of the [[Credit Union]] movement in the city and was chair of the [[University for Derry Committee]] in 1965, an unsuccessful fight to have Northern Ireland's second university established in Derry in the mid-1960s.<ref>Gerald McSheffrey, ''Planning Derry: Planning and Politics in Northern Ireland'', p. 110.</ref>\n\nHume became the youngest ever President of the [[Irish League of Credit Unions]] at age 27. He served in the role from 1964 to 1968. He once said that \"all the things I've been doing, it's the thing I'm proudest of because no movement has done more good for the people of Ireland, north and south, than the credit union movement.\"<ref>{{cite web|title=John Hume Biography and Interview|website=www.achievement.org|publisher=[[American Academy of Achievement]]|date=8 June 2002|url=https://www.achievement.org/achiever/john-hume/#interview|access-date=3 April 2019|archive-date=26 February 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190226154238/http://www.achievement.org/achiever/john-hume/#interview|url-status=live}}</ref>\n\nHume became a leading figure in the [[civil rights movements|civil rights movement]] in the late 1960s along with people such as [[Hugh Logue]].<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=NJk7DwAAQBAJ&pg=PT44|title=John Hume in America: From Londonderry to DC|publisher=Merrion Press|date=8 November 2017|last=Fitzpatrick|first=Maurice|page=44|isbn=9781911024989|access-date=19 September 2020|archive-date=23 September 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210923144852/https://books.google.com/books?id=NJk7DwAAQBAJ&pg=PT44|url-status=live}}</ref> Hume was a prominent figure in the Derry Citizens' Action Committee. The DCAC was set up in the wake of 5 October 1968 march through Derry which had caused much attention to be drawn towards the situation in Northern Ireland. The purpose of the DCAC was to make use of the publicity surrounding recent events to bring to light grievances in Derry that had been suppressed by the Unionist Government for years. The DCAC, unlike [[Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association]] (NICRA), was aimed specifically at a local campaign, improving the situation in Derry for everyone, and maintaining a peaceful stance.<ref>{{cite news|title=John Hume of DCAC|url=https://www.rte.ie/archives/exhibitions/1031-civil-rights-movement-1968-9/1036-protests-escalate/319546-john-hume-of-dcac/|access-date=3 August 2020|publisher=Raidi\u00f3 Teilif\u00eds \u00c9ireann|archive-date=13 August 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200813084947/https://www.rte.ie/archives/exhibitions/1031-civil-rights-movement-1968-9/1036-protests-escalate/319546-john-hume-of-dcac/|url-status=live}}</ref>  The committee also had a Stewards Association that was there to prevent any violence at marches or sit-downs.<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=QoAyqf7o0N8C|title=John Hume: A Biography|publisher=HarperCollinsPublishers|year=1998|last=Routledge|first=Paul|isbn=9780006387398|access-date=19 September 2020|archive-date=23 September 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210923144911/https://books.google.com/books?id=QoAyqf7o0N8C|url-status=live}}</ref>\n\n==Political career==\n[[File:President Clinton and SDLP leader John Hume.jpg|thumb|right|Hume with US president [[Bill Clinton]] in 1995]]\nHume became an [[Independent Nationalist]] member of the [[Parliament of Northern Ireland]] in 1969 at the height of the [[civil rights]] campaign. He was elected to the [[Northern Ireland Assembly (1973)|Northern Ireland Assembly]] in 1973, and served as Minister of Commerce in the short-lived power-sharing [[Executive of the 1974 Northern Ireland Assembly|Executive]] in 1974.<ref name=Nobel>{{cite web|title=John Hume \u2013 Biographical|url=https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/peace/1998/hume/biographical/|access-date=3 August 2020|publisher=Nobel Prize|archive-date=3 August 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200803095809/https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/peace/1998/hume/biographical/|url-status=live}}</ref> He stood unsuccessfully for the [[House of Commons of the United Kingdom|Westminster Parliament]] for the [[Londonderry (UK Parliament constituency)|Londonderry]] constituency in [[October 1974 United Kingdom general election|October 1974]], and was elected for [[Foyle (UK Parliament constituency)|Foyle]] in [[1983 United Kingdom general election|1983]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Parliamentary career for Mr John Hume|url=https://members.parliament.uk/member/649/career|access-date=3 August 2020|publisher=Parliament of the United Kingdom|archive-date=7 August 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200807020713/https://members.parliament.uk/member/649/career|url-status=live}}</ref>\n\nIn October 1971 he joined four Westminster MPs in a 48-hour [[hunger strike]] to protest at the [[internment]] without trial of hundreds of suspected [[Irish republican]]s. State papers that have been released under the 30 year rule that an Irish diplomat eight years later in 1979 believed Hume supported the return of internment.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.belfasttelegraph.co.uk/breaking-news/uk-ireland/top-diplomat-thought-hume-wanted-return-of-internment-14616414.html |date=30 December 2009 |archive-url=https://archive.today/2012.07.20-035516/http://www.belfasttelegraph.co.uk/breaking-news/uk-ireland/top-diplomat-thought-hume-wanted-return-of-internment-14616414.html |url-status=dead |archive-date=20 July 2012 |title=Top diplomat thought Hume wanted return of internment |work=The Daily Telegraph}}</ref>\n\nIn 1977, Hume challenged a regulation under the [[Civil Authorities (Special Powers) Act (Northern Ireland) 1922]] which allowed any soldier to disperse an assembly of three or more people. The [[Lord Chief Justice of Northern Ireland]], [[Robert Lynd Erskine Lowry|Lord Lowry]], held that the regulation was ''[[ultra vires]]'' under Section 4 of the [[Government of Ireland Act 1920]] which forbade the [[Parliament of Northern Ireland]] to make laws in respect of the army.<ref>\"Robert Lynd Erskine Lowry\"; ''ODNB''</ref>\n\nA founding member of the [[Social Democratic and Labour Party]] (SDLP), he succeeded [[Gerry Fitt]] as its leader in 1979.<ref>{{cite news|title=A mover and shaper who has made history|url=https://www.irishtimes.com/news/a-mover-and-shaper-who-has-made-history-1.141218|date=11 April 1998|access-date=3 August 2020|newspaper=The Irish Times|location=Dublin|archive-date=30 October 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201030072940/https://www.irishtimes.com/news/a-mover-and-shaper-who-has-made-history-1.141218|url-status=live}}</ref>  He also served as one of Northern Ireland's three [[Members of the European Parliament]] and served on the faculty of [[Boston College]], from which he received an honorary degree in 1995.<ref name=\"Boston College\">{{cite news|title=John Hume: Vision and legacy|url=https://www.bc.edu/bc-web/bcnews/nation-world-society/international/tribute-to-john-hume.html|date=27 April 2018|access-date=3 August 2020|publisher=Boston College|archive-date=30 October 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201030072508/https://www.bc.edu/bc-web/bcnews/nation-world-society/international/tribute-to-john-hume.html|url-status=live}}</ref>\n\nHume was directly involved in secret talks with the British government and [[Sinn F\u00e9in]], in an effort to bring Sinn F\u00e9in to the discussion table openly. The talks are speculated<ref name=Mallon2017>{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2017/nov/20/john-hume-steered-northern-ireland-peace-process|title=It was John Hume, not Sinn F\u00e9in, who steered Northern Ireland to peace|author=Seamus Mallon|date=20 November 2017|work=The Guardian|quote=''Ireland is not a romantic dream; it is not a flag; it is 4.5 million people divided into two powerful traditions.
