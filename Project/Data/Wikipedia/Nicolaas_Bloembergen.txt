In particular, Bloembergen was singled out because he \"founded a new field of science we now call [[non-linear optics]]\" by mixing \"two or more beams of laser light... in order to produce laser light of a different wave length\" and thus significantly broaden the laser spectroscopy frequency band.<ref name=\"Nobel\"/>\n\n==Early life==\nBloembergen was born in [[Dordrecht]] on March 11, 1920, where his father was a chemical engineer and executive.<ref name=\"Nobel\">Nobel Foundation [https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1981/ceremony-speech/ 1981 Nobel Presentation Speech by Professor Ingvar Lindgren] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071011080049/http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/physics/laureates/1981/bloembergen-autobio.html |date=October 11, 2007 }}</ref> He had five siblings, with his brother [[Auke Bloembergen|Auke]] later becoming a legal scholar.<ref name=histor>{{cite web |author=Rob Herber |url=http://historischekringdebilt.nl/wp-content/uploads/DBG-Nico-Bloembergen-fysicus-in-licht.pdf |title=Nico Bloembergen, fysicus in licht |language=nl |publisher=Historische Kring De Bilt |access-date=June 18, 2016 |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161012210545/http://historischekringdebilt.nl/wp-content/uploads/DBG-Nico-Bloembergen-fysicus-in-licht.pdf |archive-date=October 12, 2016 }}</ref> In 1938, Bloembergen entered the [[University of Utrecht]] to study physics. However, during [[World War II]], the [[Nazi Germany|German authorities]] closed the university and Bloembergen spent two years in hiding.<ref name=\"Nobel\"/>\n\n==Career==\n===Graduate studies===\n{{main|Nuclear Magnetic Resonance}}\nBloembergen left the war-ravaged Netherlands in 1945 to pursue graduate studies at [[Harvard University]] under Professor [[Edward Mills Purcell]].<ref name=\"EMP\">{{cite book|url=https://www.nap.edu/read/9977/chapter/12|title=Edward Mills Purcell|year=2000 |publisher=NAP.edu|doi=10.17226/9977 |isbn=978-0-309-07035-5 |access-date=September 6, 2017|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170907075314/https://www.nap.edu/read/9977/chapter/12|archive-date=September 7, 2017}}</ref> Through Purcell, Bloembergen was part of the prolific academic lineage tree of [[J. J. Thomson]], which includes many other Nobel Laureates, beginning with Thomson himself (Physics Nobel, 1906) and [[John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh|Lord Rayleigh]] (Physics Nobel, 1904), [[Ernest Rutherford]] (Chemistry Nobel 1908), [[Owen Richardson]] (Physics Nobel, 1928), and finally Purcell (Physics, Nobel 1952).<ref name=\"ADS\"/> Bloembergen's other influences include [[John Van Vleck]] (Physics Nobel, 1977) and [[Percy Bridgman]] (Physics Nobel, 1946).<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.aps.org/units/fhp/newsletters/spring2011/huber.cfm|title=John Van Vleck: Quantum Theory and Magnetism|author=David L. Hubber|publisher=APS.org|access-date=September 6, 2017|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170907033522/https://www.aps.org/units/fhp/newsletters/spring2011/huber.cfm|archive-date=September 7, 2017}}</ref>\n\nSix weeks before his arrival, Purcell and his graduate students Torrey and Pound discovered [[nuclear magnetic resonance]] (NMR).<ref name=\"EMP\"/> Bloembergen was hired to develop the first NMR machine. At Harvard he attended lectures by [[Julian Schwinger|Schwinger]], [[John Hasbrouck Van Vleck|Van Vleck]], and [[Edwin C. Kemble|Kemble]].<ref name=\"Nobel\"/> Bloembergen's NMR systems are the predecessors of modern-day [[Magnetic resonance imaging|MRI machines]], which are used to examine internal organs and tissues.<ref name=\"UUS\"/> Bloembergen's research on NMR led to an interest in [[maser]]s, which were introduced in 1953 and are the predecessors of lasers.<ref name=\"Medi\">{{cite web|url=http://www.mediatheque.lindau-nobel.org/laureates/bloembergen|title=Nicolaas Bloembergen|publisher=Mediatheque|access-date=September 6, 2017|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170907040053/http://www.mediatheque.lindau-nobel.org/laureates/bloembergen|archive-date=September 7, 2017}}</ref>\n\nBloembergen returned to the Netherlands in 1947, and submitted his thesis ''Nuclear Magnetic Relaxation'' at the [[University of Leiden]].<ref name=\"NMR\">{{cite journal |author=Nicolaas Bloembergen |author2=Edward Mills Purcell |author-link2=Edward Mills Purcell |author3=Robert V. Pound |author-link3=Robert Pound |url=http://redeye.mmrrcc.upenn.edu/mediawiki/images/8/89/A13.pdf |title=Relaxation effects in nuclear magnetic resonance absorption |journal=Physical Review |volume=73 |issue=7 |pages=679 |date=1948 |bibcode=1948PhRv...73..679B |doi=10.1103/PhysRev.73.679 |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100624181140/http://redeye.mmrrcc.upenn.edu/mediawiki/images/8/89/A13.pdf |archive-date=June 24, 2010 |doi-access=free }}</ref> This was because he had completed all the preliminary  examinations in the Netherlands, and [[Cornelis Jacobus Gorter|Cor Gorter]] of Leiden offered him a [[Postdoctoral researcher|postdoctoral appointment]] there.<ref name=\"NMR\"/> He received his [[Doctor of Philosophy|Ph.D.]] degree from Leiden in 1948, and then was a postdoc at Leiden for about a year.<ref name=\"Nobel\"/>\n\n===Professorship===\nIn 1949, he returned to Harvard as a Junior Fellow of the [[Harvard Society of Fellows|Society of Fellows]].<ref name=\"ADS\"/> In 1951, he became an associate professor; he then became Gordon McKay Professor of Applied Physics in 1957; [[Rumford Chair of Physics|Rumford Professor of Physics]] in 1974; and Gerhard Gade University Professor in 1980.<ref name=\"IEEE\">{{cite web |url=http://www.ieeeghn.org/wiki/index.php/Nicolaas_Bloembergen |title=Nicolaas Bloembergen |work=IEEE Global History Network |publisher=IEEE |access-date=July 18, 2011 |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120409051228/http://www.ieeeghn.org/wiki/index.php/Nicolaas_Bloembergen |archive-date=April 9, 2012 }}</ref> In 1990 he retired from Harvard.<ref name=\"IEEE\"/>\n\nIn addition, Bloembergen served as a visiting professor. From 1964 to 1965, Bloembergen was a visiting professor at the [[University of California, Berkeley]].<ref name=\"Nobel\"/> In 1996\u20131997, he was a visiting scientist at the College of Optical Sciences of the [[University of Arizona]]; he became a professor at Arizona in 2001.<ref>OSC Faculty [http://www.optics.arizona.edu/research/faculty/profile/nicolaas-bloembergen Nicolaas Bloembergen] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20131017120935/http://www.optics.arizona.edu/research/faculty/profile/nicolaas-bloembergen |date=October 17, 2013 }}</ref>\n\nBloembergen was a member of the Board of Sponsors of the ''[http://www.thebulletin.org Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists]'' and Honorary Editor of the ''[[Journal of Nonlinear Optical Physics & Materials]]''.<ref>World Scientific. ''Journal of Nonlinear Optical Physics & Materials''. [http://www.worldscientific.com/page/jnopm/editorial-board ''Journal Editorial Board''].</ref>\n\n===Laser spectroscopy===\n{{main|Laser spectroscopy}}\nBy 1960 while at Harvard, he experimented with [[microwave spectroscope|microwave spectroscopy]].<ref name=\"Medi\"/> Bloembergen had modified the [[maser]] of [[Charles Townes]],<ref name=\"Laser\">{{cite web|url=https://www.pddnet.com/news/2016/03/today-engineering-history-laser-patented|title=Today in Engineering History: The Laser Is Patented|date=March 22, 2016|access-date=September 6, 2017|publisher=PDDNet|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170907034554/https://www.pddnet.com/news/2016/03/today-engineering-history-laser-patented|archive-date=September 7, 2017}}</ref> and in 1956, Bloembergen developed a crystal maser, which was more powerful than the standard gaseous version.<ref name=\"NMR\"/>\n\nWith the advent of the laser, he participated in the development of the field of [[laser spectroscopy]], which allows precise observations of atomic structure using lasers. Following the development of [[second-harmonic generation]] by [[Peter Franken]] and others in 1961, Bloembergen studied how when one bombards [[matter]] with a focused and high-intensity beam of [[photons]], a new structure of matter is revealed. This he termed the study of [[nonlinear optics]]. In reflection to his work in a Dutch newspaper in 1990, Bloembergen said: \"We took a standard textbook on optics and for each section we asked ourselves what would happen if the intensity was to become very high. We were almost certain that we were bound to encounter an entirely new type of physics within that domain\".<ref name=\"UUS\">{{cite web|url=https://www.uu.nl/en/research/nicolaas-bloembergen|publisher=[[Utrecht University]]|title=Nicolaas Bloembergen|access-date=September 6, 2017|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170907033725/https://www.uu.nl/en/research/nicolaas-bloembergen|archive-date=September 7, 2017}}</ref>\n\nFrom this theoretical work, Bloembergen found ways to combine two or more laser sources consisting of photons in the [[light|visible light frequency range]] to generate a single laser source with photons of different frequencies in the [[infrared]] and [[ultraviolet]] ranges, which extends the amount of atomic detail that can be gathered from laser spectroscopy.<ref name=\"Medi\"/>\n\n==Awards==\nHe was awarded the [[Lorentz Medal]] in 1978.<ref name=\"Laser\"/> He received the Bijvoet Medal of the [[Bijvoet Center for Biomolecular Research]] of [[Utrecht University]] in 2001.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://bijvoet-center.eu/bijvoet-medal/|title=Bijvoet Medal|publisher=Bijvoet Center for Biomolecular Research|access-date=September 12, 2017|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170912191415/http://bijvoet-center.eu/bijvoet-medal/|archive-date=September 12, 2017}}</ref>\n\nBloembergen shared the 1981 [[Nobel Prize in Physics]] with [[Arthur Leonard Schawlow|Arthur Schawlow]], along with [[Kai Manne Boerje Siegbahn|Kai Siegbahn]]. The Nobel Foundation awarded Bloembergen and Schawlow \"for their contribution to the development of laser spectroscopy\".<ref name=\"Laser\"/><ref>{{cite web | url = https://www.nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/physics/laureates/1981/ | title = The Nobel Prize in Physics 1981 | publisher = [[The Nobel Foundation]] | date = 1981 | access-date = September 6, 2017 | url-status = live | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20171113062513/https://www.nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/physics/laureates/1981/ | archive-date = November 13, 2017 }}</ref>\n\n==Personal life and death==\nBloembergen met Huberta Deliana Brink (Deli) in 1948 while on vacation with his university's Physics Club. to Amsterdam.<ref name=\"nobel bio\"/> They were both [[naturalization|naturalized]] as citizens of the [[United States]] in 1958.<ref name=\"IEEE\"/> They had three children.<ref name=\"nobel
