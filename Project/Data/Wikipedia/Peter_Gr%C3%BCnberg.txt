Gleich nach Kriegsende, mit dem Einmarsch der Alliierten-Truppen, wurden alle Deutschen, so auch meine Familie, interniert. Meine Eltern kamen in ein Lager: Mein Vater Feodor ist im Lager geblieben, meine Mutter Anna dann zur Feldarbeit in das Dorf meiner Gro\u00dfeltern gekommen. Gr\u00fcnberg was a [[Catholic]].<ref>''Glauben Sie, Professor Gr\u00fcnberg, als Naturwissenschaftler an Gott?''&nbsp;\u2014 Peter Gr\u00fcnberg: ''Ja, nat\u00fcrlich. His father, a [[Russian Empire|Russia]]-born engineer who since 1928 had worked for \u0160koda, died on 27 November 1945 in Czech imprisonment and is buried in a mass grave in Pilsen which is also inscribed with ''Gr\u00fcnberg Theodor \u2020 27.&nbsp;November 1945''.<ref>''Gr\u00fcnberg Theodor \u2020 27.&nbsp;November 1945'', [http://www.westboehmen.de/tafel_pilsen.jpg westboehmen.de]</ref> His mother Anna (who died in 2002 aged 100)<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.mies-pilsen.de/gruenberg.htm |title=Nobelpreistr\u00e4ger Gr\u00fcnberg aus Pilsen |access-date=2008-01-08 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160303230102/http://www.mies-pilsen.de/gruenberg.htm |archive-date=2016-03-03 |url-status=dead }}</ref> had to work in agriculture and stayed with her parents in the Petermann<ref>Photo 2, [http://www.westboehmen.de/untersekerschan2.jpg westboehmen.de]</ref> house in Untersekerschan<ref>Photo 1, [http://www.westboehmen.de/untersekerschan.jpg westboehmen.de]</ref> (Doln\u00ed Seky\u0159any), where her children (Peter's sister was born in 1937) were brought later. The remaining Gr\u00fcnberg family, like almost all Germans, was [[Expulsion of Germans from Czechoslovakia|expelled from Czechoslovakia]] in 1946. Seven-year-old Peter came to [[Lauterbach, Hesse]] where he attended [[Gymnasium (Germany)|gymnasium]].<ref name=\"cv\">{{cite web|url=http://www.fz-juelich.de/portal/index.php?index=1191|title=Curriculum Vitae|access-date=2007-10-09|publisher=Forschungszentrum J\u00fclich|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071215154942/http://www.fz-juelich.de/portal/index.php?index=1191|archive-date=2007-12-15}}</ref>\n\nGr\u00fcnberg received his intermediate diploma in 1962 from the [[Johann Wolfgang Goethe University]] in [[Frankfurt]]. He then attended the [[Darmstadt University of Technology|Technische Universit\u00e4t Darmstadt]], where he received his BSc diploma in physics in 1966 and his Ph.D. in 1969. While there, he met and married his wife, Helma Prauser, who became a schoolteacher.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Overbye|first1=Dennis|author-link1=Dennis Overbye|title=Peter Gr\u00fcnberg, 78, Winner of an 'iPod Nobel,' Is Dead|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2018/04/12/obituaries/peter-grunberg-winner-of-an-ipod-nobel-dies-at-78.html|access-date=16 April 2018|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=13 April 2018|page=A25}}</ref> From 1969 to 1972, he did postdoctoral work at [[Carleton University]] in [[Ottawa]], Canada.<ref name=\":0\">{{Cite web|title=Gr\u00fcnberg, Peter, 1939\u2013|url=https://history.aip.org/phn/11710007.html|access-date=2021-01-19|website=history.aip.org}}</ref> He later joined the Institute for Solid State Physics at [[Forschungszentrum J\u00fclich]], [[J\u00fclich]], Germany, where he became a leading researcher in the field of thin film and multilayer magnetism until his retirement in 2004.<ref name=\"cv\" />\n\nIn 1984\u20131985 he served as Visiting Scientist at [[Argonne National Laboratories]], [[Lemont, Illinois|Lemont]], [[Illinois]], USA. From 1984 to 1992 he had [[Habilitation]] process and was a lecturer (Junior Professor), and since 1992 till 2004 a Tenured Professor (ausserplanm\u00e4ssiger Professor) at the [[University of Cologne]], Germany. He was also a Visiting Professor at the [[Tohoku University]] at [[Sendai-shi]], [[Miyagi-ken]], Japan from 1998 till 2004.\n\nIn 2007, Gr\u00fcnberg was awarded Honorary Doctorate from the [[RWTH Aachen University]], Aachen, Germany, in 2008 Honorary Doctorate from the [[Saarland University]], and from [[Gebze Institute of Technology]], and in 2009 from the [[University of Athens]].<ref name=\":0\" />\n\n==Important work==\nIn 1986 he discovered the antiparallel exchange coupling between [[ferromagnetic]] layers separated by a thin non-ferromagnetic layer, and in 1988 he discovered the [[Giant magnetoresistance|giant magnetoresistive effect]] (GMR).<ref>{{cite journal |author1=G. Binasch |author2=P. Gr\u00fcnberg |author3=F. Saurenbach |author4=W. Zinn | title=Enhanced magnetoresistance in layered magnetic structures with antiferromagnetic interlayer exchange | journal=Phys. Rev. B |year=1989 | volume=39 | issue=7 | pages=4828\u20134830 | doi=10.1103/PhysRevB.39.4828 |pmid=9948867 |bibcode = 1989PhRvB..39.4828B |doi-access=free }}</ref> GMR was simultaneously and independently discovered by [[Albert Fert]] from the [[University of Paris-Sud|Universit\u00e9 de Paris Sud]]. It has been used extensively in read heads of modern [[hard drives]]. Another application of the GMR effect is non-volatile, [[MRAM|magnetic random access memory]].\n\nApart from the Nobel Prize,  work also has been rewarded with shared prizes in the APS [[International Prize for New Materials]], the International Union of Pure and Applied Physics Magnetism Award, the Hewlett-Packard Europhysics Prize, the [[Wolf Prize in Physics]] and the 2007 [[Japan Prize]]. He won the [[German Future Prize]] for Technology and Innovation in 1998 and was named [[European Inventor of the Year]]<ref>{{cite
