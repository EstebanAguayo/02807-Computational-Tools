The analgesic [[naproxen]] is an example of a compound that is prepared industrially using the Heck reaction.\n\nFor his work in [[palladium-catalyzed coupling reactions]] and [[organic synthesis]], Heck was awarded the 2010 [[Nobel Prize in Chemistry]], shared with the Japanese chemists [[Ei-ichi Negishi]] and [[Akira Suzuki (chemist)|Akira Suzuki]].<ref name=Nobel>{{Citation | url = http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/chemistry/laureates/2010/press.html | title = Press release 6 October 2010 | publisher = Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences | access-date = October 6, 2010}}</ref>\n\n==Early life and education==\nHeck was born in [[Springfield, Massachusetts]] in 1931.<ref>{{Cite news |url=http://www.boston.com/news/local/breaking_news/2010/10/nobel_prize_win.html |title=Nobel Prize winner is Springfield native |first=Martin |last=Finucane |newspaper=[[Boston Globe]] |date=October 6, 2010 }}</ref> He moved to Los Angeles when eight years old and later attended the [[University of California, Los Angeles]] (UCLA), gaining a [[bachelor's degree]] in 1952 and then a Ph.D. in 1954 working under the supervision of [[Saul Winstein]] on the chemistry of aryl sulfonates. After postdoctoral research at the [[ETH Zurich|ETH]] in [[Zurich, Switzerland]] with [[Vladimir Prelog]], and then back at UCLA, Heck took a position with the [[Hercules Corporation]] in [[Wilmington, Delaware]] in 1956, working initially on polymer chemistry.<ref name=Negishi>{{cite journal |doi=10.1016/S0022-328X(98)01136-X |title=A profile of Professor Richard F. Heck |year=1999 |last1=Negishi |first1=Ei-Ichi |journal=Journal of Organometallic Chemistry |volume=576 |issue=1\u20132 |pages=xv-xvi }}</ref> \n\n==Career==\n[[Image:Nobel Prize 2010-Press Conference KVA-DSC 8019.jpg|250px|thumb|[[Peter Diamond]], [[Dale T. Mortensen]], [[Christopher A. Pissarides]], [[Konstantin Novoselov]], [[Andre Geim]], [[Akira Suzuki (chemist)|Akira Suzuki]], [[Ei-ichi Negishi]], and Richard Heck, Nobel Prize Laureates 2010, at a press conference at the [[Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences]] in Stockholm.]]\nAt Hercules, Heck soon became interested in [[organometallic chemistry]], including work with [[David S. Breslow]] on [[organocobalt]] reactions.<ref name=breslow1>{{cite journal |doi=10.1021/ja01488a067 |title=Allylcobalt Carbonyls |year=1960 |last1=Heck |first1=Richard F. |last2=Breslow |first2=David S. |journal=Journal of the American Chemical Society |volume=82 |issue=3 |pages=750\u2013751 }}</ref><ref name=breslow2>{{cite journal |doi=10.1021/ja01480a017|title=The Reaction of Cobalt Hydrotetracarbonyl with Olefins |year=1961 |last1=Heck |first1=Richard F. |last2=Breslow |first2=David S. |journal=Journal of the American Chemical Society |volume=83 |issue=19 |pages=4023\u20134027 }}</ref> This led to the development of the [[Heck reaction]], which began with his investigation during the late 1960s of the coupling of [[Organomercury|arylmercury]] compounds with olefins using [[palladium]] as a catalyst.<ref name=Negishi/> This work was published in a series of seven consecutive articles in the ''[[Journal of the American Chemical Society]]'' for which Heck was the sole author.<ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1021/ja01022a034 |title=Acylation, methylation, and carboxyalkylation of olefins by Group VIII metal derivatives |year=1968 |last1=Heck |first1=Richard F. |journal=Journal of the American Chemical Society |volume=90 |issue=20 |pages=5518\u20135526 }} and six further articles, pages 5526\u20135548 </ref>\n\nDuring the early 1970s, Tsutomu Mizoroki independently reported the use of the less toxic aryl halides as the coupling partner in the reaction.<ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1246/bcsj.44.581 |doi-access=free  |title=Arylation of Olefin with Aryl Iodide Catalyzed by Palladium |year=1971 |last1=Mizoroki |first1=Tsutomu |last2=Mori |first2=Kunio |last3=Ozaki |first3=Atsumu |journal=Bulletin of the Chemical Society of Japan |volume=44 |issue=2 |page=581 }}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1021/jo00979a024 |title=Palladium-catalyzed vinylic hydrogen substitution reactions with aryl, benzyl, and styryl halides |year=1972 |last1=Heck |first1=R. F. |last2=Nolley |first2=J. P. |journal=The Journal of Organic Chemistry |volume=37 |issue=14 |pages=2320\u20132322 }}</ref> Heck became a professor of chemistry at the [[University of Delaware]]'s Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry in 1971, where he continued to improve the transformation, developing it into a powerful synthetic method for organic synthesis.<ref name=Negishi/>\n\nThe importance of this reaction grew as it was taken up by others in the organic synthesis community.<ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1021/cr9903048 |title=The Heck Reaction as a Sharpening Stone of Palladium Catalysis |year=2000 |last1=Beletskaya |first1=Irina P. |last2=Cheprakov |first2=Andrei V. |journal=Chemical Reviews |volume=100 |issue=8 |pages=3009\u20133066 |pmid=11749313 }}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1039/C1CS15101K |title=The asymmetric Heck and related reactions |year=2011 |last1=Mc Cartney |first1=Dennis |last2=Guiry |first2=Patrick J. |journal=Chemical Society Reviews |volume=40 |issue=10 |pages=5122\u20135150 |pmid=21677934 }}</ref> In 1982, Heck was able to write an ''[[Organic Reactions]]'' chapter that covered all the known instances in just 45 pages.<ref>{{cite book |doi=10.1002/0471264180.or027.02 |chapter=Palladium-Catalyzed Vinylation of Organic Halides |title=Organic Reactions |year=1982 |last1=Heck |first1=Richard F. |pages=345\u2013390 |isbn=0471264180 }}</ref> By 2002, applications had grown to the extent that the ''Organic Reactions'' chapter published that year, limited to [[Intramolecular reaction|intramolecular]] Heck reactions, covered 377 pages. These reactions, a small part of the total, couple two parts of the same molecule.<ref>{{cite book |doi=10.1002/0471264180.or060.02 |chapter=The Intramolecular Heck Reaction |title=Organic Reactions |year=2002 |last1=Link |first1=J. T. |pages=157\u2013561 |isbn=0471264180 }}</ref>\nThe reaction is now one of the most widely used methods for the creation of [[carbon-carbon bond]]s in the synthesis of [[Organic compound|organic chemicals]]. It has been subject to numerous scientific review articles, including a monograph dedicated to this subject published in 2009.<ref>{{Cite book |year=2009 |editor-last= Oestreich |editor-first=Martin  |title=The Mizoroki-Heck Reaction |pages=1\u2013608  |location=[[Chichester]], United Kingdom |publisher=[[John Wiley & Sons|Wiley]] |isbn=9780470033944 |oclc=233173519 }}</ref>\n\nHeck's contributions were not limited to the activation of halides by the oxidative addition of palladium. He was the first to fully characterize a \u03c0-allyl metal complex,<ref name=breslow1/> and the first to elucidate the mechanism of alkene [[hydroformylation]].<ref name=breslow2/>\n===Palladium-catalyzed coupling reactions===\n{{See also|Palladium-catalyzed coupling reactions}}\nHeck's work set the stage for a variety of other [[palladium]]-catalyzed coupling reactions, including those of aryl halides with derivatives of [[boronic acid]] ([[Suzuki reaction|Suzuki\u2013Miyaura coupling]]), [[organotin]] reagents ([[Stille reaction|Stille coupling]]), [[organomagnesium]] compounds ([[Kumada coupling|Kumada-Corriu coupling]]), [[silane]]s ([[Hiyama coupling]]), and [[organozinc]]s ([[Negishi coupling]]), as well as with amines ([[Buchwald\u2013Hartwig amination]]) and [[alcohols]].<ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1002/anie.201107017  |title=Palladium-Catalyzed Cross-Coupling: A Historical Contextual Perspective to the 2010 Nobel Prize |year=2012 |last1=Johansson Seechurn |first1=Carin C. C. |last2=Kitching |first2=Matthew O. |last3=Colacot |first3=Thomas J. |last4=Snieckus |first4=Victor |journal=Angewandte Chemie International Edition |volume=51 |issue=21 |pages=5062\u20135085 |pmid=22573393 }}</ref> These palladium-catalyzed coupling reactions are now widely practiced in organic synthesis, including for the manufacture of [[pharmaceutical drug]]s such as [[naproxen]].<ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1021/op960009e |title=Twenty Years of Naproxen Technology |year=1997 |last1=Harrington |first1=Peter J. |last2=Lodewijk |first2=Eric |journal=Organic Process Research & Development |volume=1 |pages=72\u201376 }}</ref>\n\nOf the several reactions developed by Heck, the greatest societal impact has been from the palladium-catalyzed coupling of an alkyne with an aryl [[halide]]. This is the reaction that was used to couple fluorescent dyes to DNA bases, allowing the automation of [[DNA sequencing]] and the examination of the [[human genome]]; the reaction also allows biologically important proteins to be tracked.<ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1002/cbic.200600432  |title=Site-Specific Functionalization of Proteins by Organopalladium Reactions |year=2007 |last1=Kodama |first1=Koichiro |last2=Fukuzawa |first2=Seketsu |last3=Nakayama |first3=Hiroshi |last4=Sakamoto |first4=Kensaku |last5=Kigawa |first5=Takanori |last6=Yabuki |first6=Takashi |last7=Matsuda |first7=Natsuko |last8=Shirouzu |first8=Mikako |last9=Takio |first9=Koji |last10=Yokoyama |first10=Shigeyuki |last11=Tachibana |first11=Kazuo |journal=ChemBioChem |volume=8 |issue=2 |pages=232\u2013238 |pmid=17195252 |s2cid=46474691 }}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1021/cb500443x |doi-access=free |title=Fast and Sequence-Specific Palladium-Mediated Cross-Coupling Reaction Identified from Phage Display |year=2014 |last1=Lim |first1=Reyna K. V. |last2=Li |first2=Nan |last3=Ramil |first3=Carlo P. |last4=Lin |first4=Qing |journal=ACS Chemical Biology |volume=9 |issue=9 |pages=2139\u20132148 |pmid=25025771 |pmc=4168780 }}</ref> In Sonogashira's original report of what is now known as the [[Sonogashira coupling]], his group modified an alkyne coupling procedure previously reported by Heck, by adding a copper(I) salt.<ref name=\"PCCR\">{{cite journal |doi=10.1016/S0040-4039(00)91094-3  |title=A convenient synthesis of acetylenes: Catalytic substitutions of acetylenic hydrogen with bromoalkenes, iodoarenes and bromopyridines |year=1975 |last1=Sonogashira |first1=Kenkichi |last2=Tohda |first2=Yasuo |last3=Hagihara |first3=Nobue |journal=Tetrahedron Letters |volume=16 |issue=50 |pages=4467\u20134470 }}</ref>\n\n==Later life and death==\nHeck retired from the University of Delaware in 1989, where he became the Willis F. Harrington Professor Emeritus in the Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry. Its  annual lectureship was named in his honor in 2004. In 2005, he was awarded the [[Wallace H. Carothers]] Award, which recognizes creative applications of chemistry that have had substantial commercial impact. He was awarded the 2006 [[Herbert C. Brown]] Award for creative research in synthetic methods.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www1.udel.edu/udaily/2016/oct/InMemoriam101015.html |title=Richard Heck, professor emeritus and Nobel laureate, dies |website=udel.edu |date=October 10, 2015 |access-date=August 22, 2022}}</ref><ref name=acs>{{cite journal |periodical=[[Chemical & Engineering News|C&EN]] |volume=84 |issue=6 |pages=34\u201338 |date=February 6, 2006 |title=2006 ACS National Award Winners |url=http://pubs.acs.org/email/cen/html/020906105850.html#5 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070806005818/http://pubs.acs.org/email/cen/html/020906105850.html |archive-date=August 6, 2007 |url-status=dead }}.</ref> On October 6, 2010, the Swedish Royal Academy of Sciences awarded Heck the Nobel Prize in Chemistry, which he shared with [[Ei-ichi Negishi]] and [[Akira Suzuki (chemist)|Akira Suzuki]] \"for palladium-catalyzed cross couplings in organic synthesis\".<ref name=Nobel/><ref name=\"Nobel interview\">{{cite web |url=http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/chemistry/laureates/2010/heck-telephone.html |title=Richard F. He had moved to [[Quezon City]], [[Philippines]] after retirement, with his wife, Socorro Nardo-Heck.
