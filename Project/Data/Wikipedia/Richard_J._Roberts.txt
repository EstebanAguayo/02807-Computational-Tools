In Bath, he attended [[City of Bath Boys' School]].<ref name=nobelbio/> As a child he at first wanted to be a [[detective]] and then, when given a [[chemistry set]], a [[chemist]]. In 1965 he graduated from the [[University of Sheffield]] with a [[Bachelor of Science]] degree in [[Chemistry]] followed by a [[PhD]] in 1969.<ref name=\"whoswho\"/> His thesis involved [[phytochemical]] studies of [[neoflavonoid]]s and [[isoflavonoid]]s.<ref name=\"robertsphd\">{{cite thesis |degree=PhD |first=Richard John|last=Roberts |title=Phytochemical studies involving neoflavanoids and isoflavanoids |website=copac.jisc.ac.uk|publisher=University of Sheffield |year=1969 |url=http://copac.jisc.ac.uk/id/36827249?style=html|oclc=270832404}}</ref><ref name=ORCID>Richard J. Roberts {{ORCID|0000-0002-4348-0169}}</ref>\n\n==Career and research==\nDuring 1969\u20131972, he did [[postdoctoral research]] at [[Harvard University]].<ref name=\"nobelbio\">{{cite web\n| url = https://www.nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/medicine/laureates/1993/roberts-bio.html\n| title = Richard J. In 1977, he published his discovery of RNA splicing.<ref name=adeno/> In 1992, he moved to [[New England Biolabs]].<ref name=nobelbio/> The following year, he shared a Nobel Prize with his former colleague at Cold Spring Harbor [[Phillip Allen Sharp]].<ref name=\"KlimasauskasKumar1994\">{{cite journal|last1=Klimasauskas|first1=Saulius|last2=Kumar|first2=Sanjay|last3=Roberts|first3=Richard J.|last4=Cheng|first4=Xiaodong|title=Hhal methyltransferase flips its target base out of the DNA helix|journal=Cell|volume=76|issue=2|year=1994|pages=357\u2013369|pmid=8293469|doi=10.1016/0092-8674(94)90342-5|s2cid=23161543}}</ref>\n\nRoberts's discovery of the [[alternative splicing]] of genes, in particular, has had a profound impact on the study and applications of molecular biology.<ref name=frs/> The realisation that individual genes could exist as separate, disconnected segments within longer strands of DNA first arose in his 1977 study of [[adenovirus]],<ref name=adeno/> one of the viruses responsible for causing the common cold. Robert's research in this field resulted in a fundamental shift in our understanding of [[genetics]], and has led to the discovery of split genes in higher organisms, including human beings.<ref
