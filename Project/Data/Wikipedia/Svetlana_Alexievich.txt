mother,<ref>{{cite news|title=Remembering the Great Patriotic War was a political Svetlana Alexievich grew up in Belarus. After graduating from high school she worked as a reporter in several local newspapers. In 1972 she graduated from [[Belarusian State University]] and became a correspondent for the literary magazine ''Nyoman'' in [[Minsk]] (1976).<ref>[http://who.bdg.by/obj.php?&kod=262 Brief biography of Svetlana Alexievich (Russian)] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140918014331/http://who.bdg.by/obj.php?&kod=262 |date=2014-09-18 }}, from ''Who is who in Belarus''</ref> <!-- Do not add any information that she wrote about carp-fishing - this is an admitted hoax (https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2022/mar/30/techscape-fake-news-misinformation) -->\n\n[[File:FM-2019-1-3-6-194 Niggl-Radloff-B-Portrait-Villa-Waldberta-ONLINE.jpg|thumb|Alexievich as artist in residence at Bavarian Villa Waldberta in the 1990s]]\n\nIn a 2015 interview, she mentioned early influences: \"I explored the world through people like [[Hanna Krall]] and [[Ryszard Kapu\u015bci\u0144ski]].\"<ref>{{cite web |title=2015 Nobel Laureate Alexievich Discusses Polish Influences |url=https://culture.pl/en/article/2015-nobel-laureate-alexievich-discusses-polish-influences |website=[[Culture.pl]] | date=October 13, 2015 |access-date=17 April 2022}}</ref> During her career in journalism, Alexievich specialized in crafting narratives based on witness testimonies.  [[Soviet\u2013Afghan War|Afghan War]],<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.latimes.com/books/la-ca-jc-svetlana-alexievich-20151206-story.html|title=Svetlana Alexievich's 'Zinky Boys' gives voice to the Alexievich was tried a number of times between 1992 and 1996. After [[political persecution]] by the [[Alexander Lukashenko|Lukashenko]] administration,<ref>[http://www.lannan.org/bios/svetlana-alexievich Biography of Aleksievich] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151011220156/http://www.lannan.org/bios/svetlana-alexievich |date=2015-10-11 }} at Lannan Foundation website</ref> she left Belarus in 2000.<ref>{{cite web|title=Svetlana Alexievich: The Empire Will Not Pass Away Without Bloodshed|url=http://belarusians.co.uk/2014/09/18/svetlana-alexievich-the-empire-will-not-pass-away-without-bloodshed/|date=18 September 2014|website=www.belarusians.co.uk|access-date=8 October 2015|url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150919065259/http://belarusians.co.uk/2014/09/18/svetlana-alexievich-the-empire-will-not-pass-away-without-bloodshed/|archive-date=19 September 2015}}</ref> The [[International Cities of Refuge Network]] offered her sanctuary, and during the following decade she lived in Paris, [[Gothenburg]] and Berlin. The course of the Soviet-Afghan War (1979-1989) is told through emotive personal testimony from unnamed participants of the war; from nurses, to commissioned officers and pilots, mothers and widows. Each provides an excerpt of the Soviet-Afghan War which was disguised in the face of criticism first as political support, then intervention, and finally humanitarian aid to the Afghan people. Alexievich writes at the beginning of the book: \n{{quote|After the great wars of the twentieth century and the mass deaths, writing about the modern (small) wars, like the war in Afghanistan, requires different ethical and metaphysical stances. In \"Boys in Zinc\", Alexievich calls herself 'a historian of the untraceable' and 'strive[s] desperately (from book to book) to do one thing - reduce history to the human being.'<ref> Alexievich, \"Boys in Zinc\" p.18</ref> She brings brutally honest accounts of the war to lay at the feet of the Soviet people, but claims no heroism for herself: 'I went [to watch them assemble pieces of boys blown up by an anti-tank mine] and there was nothing heroic about it, because I fainted there. Perhaps it was from the heat, perhaps from the shock. I want to be honest.'<ref> Alexievich, \"Boys in Zinc\", p. 20</ref> The monologues which make up the book are honest (if edited for clarity) reproductions of the oral histories Alexievich collected, including those who perhaps did not understand her purpose: 'What's your book for? Who's it for? None of us who came back from there will like it anyway. How can you possibly tell people how it was? The dead camels and dead men lying in a single pool of blood, with their blood mingled together. Who wants that?'<ref>Alexievich, \"Boys in Zinc\" p.30</ref> Alexievich was brought to trial in Minsk between 1992 and 1996, accused of distorting and falsifying the testimony of Afghan veterans and their mothers who were 'offended [...] that their boys were portrayed exclusively as soulless killer-robots, pillagers, drug addicts and rapists...' <ref>Griegoriev. \"Vecherny Minsk\", 2 June 1992</ref> The trial, while apparently defending the honour of the army and veterans, is widely seen as an attempt to preserve old ideology in post-communist Belarus.
