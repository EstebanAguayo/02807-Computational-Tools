Media AB |access-date=5 October 2018}}</ref> is a Japanese [[physician-scientist]] and [[immunologist]]. He won the 2018 [[Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine]] and is  best known for his identification of [[programmed cell death protein 1]] (PD-1).<ref>{{Cite Q|Q24293504}}</ref>\nHe is also known for his molecular identification of [[cytokines]]: [[Interleukin 4|IL-4]] and [[Interleukin 5|IL-5]],<ref>{{Cite Q|Q34106729|doi-access=free}}</ref> as well as the discovery of [[activation-induced cytidine deaminase]] (AID) that is essential for [[class switch recombination]] and [[somatic hypermutation]].<ref name=\"koch\">{{cite web|url=http://www.robert-koch-stiftung.de/index.php?article_id=90&clang=1|title=Robert Koch Stiftung - Christine Goffinet|website=www.robert-koch-stiftung.de}}</ref>\n\nHe was elected as a foreign associate of the [[National Academy of Sciences]] of the [[United States]] (2001), as a member of [[German Academy of Natural Scientists Leopoldina]] (2003), and also as a member of the [[Japan Academy]] (2005).\n\nIn 2018, he was awarded the [[Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine]] along with [[James P. Allison]].<ref name=\"TGUK\">{{cite web |last1=Hannah |first1=Devlin |title=James P Allison and Tasuku Honjo win Nobel prize for medicine |url=https://www.theguardian.com/science/2018/oct/01/james-p-allison-and-tasuku-honjo-win-nobel-prize-for-medicine |work=The Guardian |access-date=1 October 2018|date=October 2018 }}</ref> He and Allison together had won the 2014 [[Tang Prize|Tang Prize in Biopharmaceutical Science]] for the same achievement.<ref name=\"TP\">{{cite web |url=http://www.tang-prize.org/en/owner.php?cat=11&y=2 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171020051653/http://www.tang-prize.org/en/owner.php?cat=11&y=2 |archive-date=2017-10-20 |title=2014 Tang Prize in Biopharmaceutical Science |access-date=2016-06-18 |url-status=live }}</ref>\n\n== Life and career ==\n[[File:Tasuku Honjo EM1B5529 (46157227432).jpg|thumb|left|200px|At Nobel press conference in Stockholm, December 2018]]\nHonjo was born in Kyoto in 1942. He completed his [[Medical degree|M.D.]] degree in 1966 from the Faculty of Medicine, [[Kyoto University]], where in 1975 he received his [[Doctor of Philosophy|Ph.D.]] degree in Medical Chemistry under the supervision of [[Yasutomi Nishizuka]] and [[Osamu Hayaishi]].<ref name=\"JBIO\">\"{{cite web|url=http://www.brh.co.jp/s_library/j_site/scientistweb/no37/|title=\u514d\u75ab\u306e\u3057\u304f\u307f\u306b\u9b45\u305b\u3089\u308c\u3066\uff0d\u4f55\u3054\u3068\u306b\u3082\u4e3b\u4f53\u7684\u306b\u6311\u3080|language=ja}}</ref>\n\nHonjo was a visiting fellow at the Department of Embryology, [[Carnegie Institution for Science|Carnegie Institution of Washington]], from 1971 to 1973.  He then moved to the U.S. [[National Institutes of Health]] (NIH) in Bethesda, Maryland, where he studied the genetic basis for the immune response at the [[Eunice Kennedy Shriver National Institute of Child Health and Human Development|National Institute of Child Health and Human Development]] as a fellow between 1973 and 1977, followed by many years as an NIH Fogarty Scholar in Residence starting in 1992.  During part of this time, Honjo also was an assistant professor at the [[Tokyo Medical University|Faculty of Medicine, University of Tokyo]], between 1974 and 1979; a professor in the Department of Genetics, [[Osaka University|Osaka University School of Medicine]], between 1979 and 1984; and professor in the Department of Medical Chemistry, Kyoto University Faculty of Medicine, from 1984 to 2005.  Since 2005 Honjo has been a professor in Department of Immunology and Genomic Medicine, Kyoto University Faculty of Medicine.<ref name=\"JBIO\"/> He was the President of [[University of Shizuoka|Shizuoka Prefecture Public University Corporation]] from 2012 to 2017.\n\nHe is a member of the Japanese Society for Immunology and served as its president between 1999 and 2000.  Honjo is also an honorary member of [[American Association of Immunologists]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.aai.org/About/Allison-Honjo-Awarded-2018-Nobel-Prize|title=AAI Members Awarded the 2018 Nobel Prizein Physiology or Medicine|publisher=The American Association of Immunologists|access-date=October 4, 2018}}</ref>  In 2017 he became Deputy Director-General and Distinguished Professor of Kyoto University Institute for Advanced Study (KUIAS).<ref name=\"KP\" />\n\n===[[COVID-19 pandemic in India|COVID]]-19 pandemic===\nDuring the [[COVID-19 pandemic]], a false claim that Honjo believed said he was 'greatly saddened' that his name had been used to spread 'false accusations and misinformation'.<ref name=\"BBC News 2 May 2020\"/>\n\n== Contribution ==\n[[File:11 Hegasy CTLA4 PD1 Immunotherapy.png|thumb|left|200px|Cancer therapy by inhibition of negative immune regulation (CTLA4, PD1)]]\nHonjo has established the basic conceptual framework of [[class switch recombination]].<ref name=\"koch\"/> He presented a model explaining antibody gene rearrangement in class switch and, between 1980 and 1982, verified its validity by elucidating its [[DNA structure]].<ref>{{Cite Q|Q34270140}}</ref> He succeeded in [[cDNA clone|cDNA clonings]] of [[Interleukin 4|IL-4]]<ref>{{Cite Q|Q34161873}}</ref> and [[Interleukin 5|IL-5]]<ref>{{Cite Q|Q34162731}}</ref> cytokines involved in class switching and [[Interleukin 2|IL-2]] receptor alpha chain in 1986, and went on further to discover [[Activation-induced cytidine deaminase|AID]]<ref>{{Cite Q|Q29547201|doi-access=free}}</ref> in 2000, demonstrating its importance in class switch recombination and [[somatic hypermutation]].\n\nIn 1992, Honjo first identified [[Programmed cell death protein 1|PD-1]] as an inducible gene on activated T-lymphocytes, and this discovery significantly contributed to the establishment of [[cancer immunotherapy]] principle by PD-1 blockade.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ms-fund.keio.ac.jp/prize/winner/prize2016.html |title=The Keio Medical Science Prize Laureates 2016 |publisher=Ms-fund.keio.ac.jp |access-date=2018-10-01}}</ref>\n\n== Awards ==\n[[File:Tasuku Honjo Susumu Nakanishi Ikuta Takagi Shinzo Abe Shunichi Iwasaki and Goichi Oda 20131103.jpg|thumb|200px|[[Shun-ichi Iwasaki|Shun'ichi In 2018, he shared the [[Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine]] with American immunologist  [[James P. Allison]].
