Berkley Professor of [[Economics]] and Business at [[New York University]].<ref>{{cite web | title=NYU Bio: Thomas J. Sargent | url=http://www.stern.nyu.edu/faculty/bio/thomas-sargent | publisher=[[NYU Stern]] | access-date=3 July 2016}}</ref> He specializes in the fields of [[macroeconomics]], [[monetary economics]], and [[time series]] [[econometrics]]. As of 2020, he ranks as the 29th most cited economist in the world.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://ideas.repec.org/top/top.person.all.html |title=Economist Rankings at IDEAS |publisher=Ideas.repec.org |access-date=2011-10-10}}</ref> He was awarded the [[Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences|Nobel Memorial Prize in Economics]] in 2011 together with [[Christopher A. Sims]] for their \"empirical research on cause and effect in the macroeconomy\".<ref>{{cite press release | url=https://www.nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/economic-sciences/laureates/2011/press.html | title=The Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel 2011 | publisher=[[Nobel Prize]] | date=10 October 2011}}</ref>\n\n==Education==\nSargent graduated from Monrovia High School.<ref>{{cite news | url=http://www.pasadenastarnews.com/general-news/20111010/monrovia-high-grad-wins-nobel-prize-in-economics | title=Monrovia High grad wins Nobel Prize in economics | publisher=[[Pasadena Star-News]] | date=10 October 2011 | url-status=dead | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150924064948/http://www.pasadenastarnews.com/general-news/20111010/monrovia-high-grad-wins-nobel-prize-in-economics | archive-date=24 September 2015 }}</ref> He earned his B.A. from the [[University of California, Berkeley]] in 1964, being the University Medalist as Most Distinguished Scholar in Class of 1964, and his PhD from [[Harvard University|Harvard]] in 1968, under supervision of [[John R. Meyer]].<ref>{{Cite book |first=Esther-Mirjam |last=Sent |title=The Evolving Rationality of Rational Expectations: An Assessment of Thomas Sargent's Achievements |publisher=Cambridge University Press |year=2006 |page=35 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=qUvmlY2CcQgC&pg=PA35 |isbn=9780521027717 }}</ref> Sargent's classmates at Harvard included [[Christopher A. Sims]]. After serving in the U.S. Army as a first lieutenant and captain, he moved on to teaching.<ref name=\"stern.nyu.edu\">URL:https://www.stern.nyu.edu/faculty/bio/thomas-sargent</ref> He held teaching positions at the [[University of Pennsylvania]] (1970\u201371), [[University of Minnesota]] (1971\u201387), [[University of Chicago]] (1991\u201398), [[Stanford University]] (1998\u20132002) and [[Princeton University]] (2009), and is currently a professor of economics at [[New York University]] (since 2002).  He previously held the position of President of the [[American Economic Association]] and the [[Econometric Society]] where he has been a fellow since 1976.<ref name=\"stern.nyu.edu\"/> In 1983, Sargent was elected to the National Academy of Sciences and also the [[American Academy of Arts and Sciences]].<ref name=AAAS>{{cite web|title=Book of Members, 1780\u20132011: Chapter S|url=http://www.amacad.org/publications/BookofMembers/ChapterS.pdf|publisher=American Academy of Arts and Sciences|access-date=13 October 2011}}</ref> He has been a senior fellow of the [[Hoover Institution]] at [[Stanford University]] since 1987.\n\n==Professional contributions==\nSargent is one of the leaders of the \"[[rational expectations]] revolution,\" which argues that the people being modeled by economists can predict the future, or the probability of future outcomes, at least as well as the economist can with his model. Rational expectations was introduced into economics by [[John Muth]],<ref name=\"muth\">{{Cite journal | last1 = Muth | first1 = J. F. | title = Rational Expectations and the Theory of Price Movements | journal = Econometrica | volume = 29 | issue = 3 | pages = 315\u201335 | year = 1961 | doi = 10.2307/1909635| jstor = 1909635}}</ref> then [[Robert Lucas, Jr.]], and [[Edward C. Prescott]] took it much farther. By some works written in close collaboration with Lucas and [[Neil Wallace]], Thomas J. Sargent could fundamentally contribute to the evolution of [[new classical macroeconomics]].<ref>{{cite book |last=Galb\u00e1cs |first=Peter |title=The Theory of New Classical Macroeconomics. A Positive Critique |location=Heidelberg/New York/Dordrecht/London |publisher=Springer |year=2015 |isbn= 978-3-319-17578-2|doi=10.1007/978-3-319-17578-2 |series=Contributions to Economics }}</ref>\n\nSargent's main contributions to rational expectations were these:\n* trace the implications of rational expectations, with Wallace, for alternative monetary-policy instruments and rules on output stability and price determinacy.<ref>Sargent, Thomas J., and Neil Wallace, 1975. \"'Rational' Expectations, the Optimal Monetary Instrument, and the Optimal Money Supply Rule,\" ''Journal of Political Economy'', 83(2), pp. [http://isites.harvard.edu/fs/docs/icb.topic500592.files/sargent%20wallace.pdf  pp. 241\u201354].</ref>\n* help make the theory of rational expectations statistically operational.<ref>{{Cite journal | doi = 10.1086/260963 | jstor = 1833309 | last1 = Sargent | first1 = T. J. | title = Interpreting Economic Time Series | journal = The Journal of Political Economy | volume = 89 | issue = 2 | pages = 213\u201348 | year = 1981| s2cid = 152875567 | url = http://minneapolisfed.org/research/sr/sr58.pdf }}</ref>\n* provide some early examples of rational expectations models of the [[Phillips curve]], the [[yield curve|term structure of interest rates]], and the [[demand for money]] during [[hyperinflation]]s.<ref>{{cite journal |last1=Sargent |first1=Thomas J. |last2=Wallace |first2=Neil |title=Rational Expectations and the Theory of Economic Policy |journal=Journal of Monetary Economics |volume=2 |issue=2 |pages=169\u201383 |year=1976 |url=http://www.policonomics.com/wp-content/uploads/Rational-Expectations-and-the-Theory-of-Economic-Policy.pdf |doi=10.1016/0304-3932(76)90032-5 }}{{dead link|date=December 2017 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal | last1 = Sargent | first1 = T. (1983). \"The Ends of Four Big Inflations\" in: ''Inflation: Causes and Effects'', ed. by Robert E. economics.\n\nSargent went on to refine or extend rational expectations reasoning by further:\n* studying the conditions under which systems with [[bounded rationality]] of agents and [[adaptive learning|adaptive learners]] converge to rational expectations.<ref>{{cite book |title= Bounded Rationality in Macroeconomics|last=Sargent|first=Thomas J.|year=1993 |publisher=Oxford University Press [https://www.questia.com/library/book/bounded-rationality-in-macroeconomics-thomas-j-sargent-by-thomas-j-sargent.jsp links].</ref><ref>{{Cite journal | last1 = Marcet | first1 = A. | title = Convergence of least squares learning mechanisms in self-referential linear stochastic models*1 | journal = Journal of Economic Theory | volume = 48 | issue = 2 | pages = 337\u201368 | year = 1989 | studying contexts with [[Lars Peter Hansen]] in which decision makers do not trust their probability model. In particular, Hansen and Sargent adapt and extend methods from  [[Robust control|robust control theory]].<ref name=\"robustnessbook\">{{cite book |title= Robustness|last=Hansen |first=Lars Peter |author2=Sargent, Thomas J. |year=2008 |publisher=Princeton University Press |isbn=978-0-691-11442-2 }}</ref>\n\nSargent has also been a pioneer in introducing [[recursive economics]] to academic study, especially for macroeconomic issues such as unemployment, fiscal and monetary policy, and growth. His series of textbooks, co-authored with [[Lars Ljungqvist]], are seminal in the contemporary graduate economics curriculum.\n\nSargent has pursued a research program with Ljungqvist<ref>{{cite web|title = Lars Ljungqvist|url = http://www2.hhs.se/personal/ljungqvist/|url-status = dead|archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20111229215440/http://www2.hhs.se/personal/Ljungqvist/|archive-date = 2011-12-29}}</ref> designed to understand determinants of differences in [[unemployment|unemployment outcomes]] in Europe and the United States during the last 30 years. The two key questions the program addresses are why, in the 1950s and 1960s, unemployment was systematically lower in Europe than in the United States and why, for two and a half decades after 1980,\nunemployment has been systematically higher in Europe than in the United States. In \"Two Questions about European Unemployment,\" the answer is that \"Europe has stronger employment protection despite also having had more generous government supplied unemployment compensation\".\" While the institutional differences remained the same over this time period, the microeconomic environment for workers changed, with a higher risk of [[human capital]] depreciation in the 1980s.<ref>{{Cite journal | last1 = Ljungqvist | first1 = L. | last2 = Sargent | first2 = T. J. | doi = 10.1111/j.0012-9682.2008.00816.x | title = Two Questions about European Unemployment | journal = Econometrica | volume = 76 | page = 1 | year = 2008 }}</ref>\n\nIn 1997, he won the [[Nemmers Prize in Economics]]<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.stern.nyu.edu/faculty/bio/thomas-sargent|title = NYU Stern - Thomas Sargent - William R. Berkley Professor of Economics and Business}}</ref>\n\nIn 2011, he was awarded the [[NAS Award for Scientific Reviewing]] from the [[United States National Academy of Sciences|National Academy of Sciences]]<ref name=Reviewing>{{cite web|title=NAS Award for Scientific Reviewing|url=http://www.nasonline.org/site/PageServer?pagename=AWARDS_scirev|publisher=National Academy of Sciences|access-date=27 February 2011|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110318130922/http://www.nasonline.org/site/PageServer?pagename=AWARDS_scirev|archive-date=18 March 2011}}</ref> and, in September, he became the recipient of the 2011 [[CME Group]]-[[Mathematical Sciences Research Institute|MSRI]] Prize in Innovative Quantitative Applications.<ref>{{cite web|title=2011 CME Group-MSRI Prize|url=http://www.msri.org/web/msri/public-events/show/-/event/Em9210}}</ref>\n\nSargent is known as a devoted teacher. Among his PhD advisees are men and women at the forefront of macroeconomic research {{Who|date=December 2014}}. Sargent's reading group at Stanford and NYU is a famous institution among graduate students in economics.<ref>{{cite web|title=Conference in Honor of Tom Sargent's Nobel Prize in Economics and Reading Group Reunion |url=http://www.stern.nyu.edu/experience-stern/about/departments-centers-initiatives/centers-of-research/global-economy-business/events/past-events/thomas-sargent/index.htm}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Videos of Reading Group Conference| website=[[YouTube]] | url=https://www.youtube.com/user/nyusternecon#g/u }}</ref>\n\nIn 2016, Sargent helped found the non-profit [https://quantecon.org/ QuantEcon] project, which is dedicated to the development and documentation of modern open source computational tools for economics, econometrics, and decision making.<ref>{{cite web|title=QuantEcon|url=https://www.quantecon.org|website=QuantEcon|access-date=4 August 2017}}</ref>\n\nCurrently he is director of the Sargent Institute of Quantitative Economics and Finance (SIQEF) at Peking University HSBC Business School in Shenzhen.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://siqef.phbs.pku.edu.cn/en/2019/ONews_0719/12.html|title = Interview with Thomas Sargent on the PHD and Elite MA Programs at PHBS - News - Sargent Institute of Quantitative Economics of Finance}}</ref>\n\n==Nobel Prize==\n[[File:Nobel Thomas J.
