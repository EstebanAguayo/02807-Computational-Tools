15 April 1931 \u2013 26 March 2015) was a Swedish poet, psychologist and translator.<ref>[http://www.svt.se/nyheter/inrikes/tomas-transtromer-ar-dod Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer \u00e4r d\u00f6d.] SVT Nyheter, 27 March 2015.</ref> His poems captured the long Swedish winters, the rhythm of the seasons and the palpable, atmospheric beauty of nature.<ref name=\"nyt\"/> Transtr\u00f6mer's work is also characterized by a sense of mystery and wonder underlying the routine of everyday life, a quality which often gives his poems a religious dimension.<ref name=\"PE\">{{cite news |title=Straight into the Invisible: A Swedish Poet's Explorations'' |first=Stephan |last=Salisbury |url=http://articles.philly.com/1987-03-25/news/26223103_1_psychologist-explorations-poets/3 |newspaper=The Philadelphia Inquirer|year=1987 |access-date=20 October 2011}}</ref> He has been described as a [[Christians|Christian]] poet.<ref name=\"CPR\">{{cite news |title=Anchor in the Shadows: Review of ''The Great Enigma: New Collected Poems'' |first=Bill |last=Coyle |url=http://www.cprw.com/Coyle/transtromer.htm |newspaper=[[Contemporary Poetry Review]] |year=2009 |access-date=20 October 2011}}</ref>\n\nTranstr\u00f6mer is acclaimed as one of the most important Scandinavian writers since the [[World War II|Second World War]]. Critics praised his poetry for its accessibility, even in translation.<ref name=\"nyt\"/> His poetry has been translated into over 60 languages.<ref name=\"nyt\">{{cite news |title=Swedish Poet Wins Nobel Prize for Literature |first=Julie |last=Bosman |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2011/10/07/arts/swedish-poet-wins-nobel-prize-for-literature.html?_r=1&hp |newspaper=[[The New York Times]] |date=6 October 2011 |access-date=6 October 2011}}</ref> He was the recipient of the 1990 [[Neustadt International Prize for Literature]], the 2004 [[Nonino#Winners|International Nonino Prize]], and the [[2011 Nobel Prize in Literature]].<ref name=\"ap nobel\">{{cite web|url=https://www.nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/literature/laureates/2011/press.html |title=The Nobel Prize in Literature 2011 \u2013 Press Release |publisher=Nobelprize.org |access-date=6 October 2011}}</ref>\n\n==Life and work==\n\n===Early life===\nTranstr\u00f6mer was born in [[Stockholm]] in 1931 and raised by his mother Helmy, a schoolteacher, following her divorce from his father, G\u00f6sta Transtr\u00f6mer, an editor.<ref name=\"ap nobel\"/><ref name=\"guardian\">{{cite news |title=Nobel prize for literature goes to Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer |first1=Richard |last1=Lea |first2=Alison |last2=Flood |url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2011/oct/06/nobel-prize-literature-tomas-transtromer?newsfeed=true |newspaper=[[The Guardian]] |date=6 October 2011 |access-date=6 October 2011}}</ref> He received his secondary education at the [[S\u00f6dra Latin]] [[Gymnasium (school)|Gymnasium]] in Stockholm, where he began writing poetry. In addition to selected journal publications, his first collection of poems, ''[[17 Poems]]'', was published in 1954. He continued his education at [[Stockholm University]], graduating as a psychologist in 1956 with additional studies in history, religion and literature.<ref name=\"wp nobel\">{{cite news | url = https://www.washingtonpost.com/entertainment/books/swedish-poet-tomas-transtromer-wins-nobel-literature-prize/2011/10/06/gIQAJ9YiPL_story.html | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20111006154906/http://www.washingtonpost.com/entertainment/books/swedish-poet-tomas-transtromer-wins-nobel-literature-prize/2011/10/06/gIQAJ9YiPL_story.html | url-status = dead | archive-date = 6 October 2011 | title = Swedish poet Tomas Transtromer wins Nobel literature prize for 'condensed, translucent' works | agency = Associated Press | date =6 October 2011 | access-date  = 6 October 2011 }}</ref> Between 1960 and 1966, Transtr\u00f6mer split his time between working as a psychologist at the Roxtuna center for juvenile delinquents ([[:sv:Anstalten Roxtuna|sv]]) and writing poetry.<ref name=\"ap nobel\"/> He also worked as a psychologist at the Labor Market Institute in [[V\u00e4ster\u00e5s]] from 1965 to 1990.<ref>{{cite web|title=Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer Biographical|url=https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/literature/2011/transtromer/biographical|website=nobelprize.org|access-date=1 March 2022}}</ref>\n\n===Poetry===\nTranstr\u00f6mer is considered to be one of the \"most influential Scandinavian poet[s] of recent decades\".<ref name=\"ap nobel\"/> Transtr\u00f6mer published 15 collected works over his extensive career, which have been translated into over 60 languages.<ref name=\"ap nobel\"/> An English translation by [[Robin Fulton]] of his entire body of work, ''New Collected Poems'', was published in the UK in 1987 and expanded in 1997. Following the publication of ''The Great Enigma'', Fulton's edition was further expanded into ''The Great Enigma: New Collected Poems,'' published in the US in 2006 and as an updated edition of ''New Collected Poems''<ref>{{cite news |title=New Collected Poems by Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer \u2013 review |first=Paul |last=Batchelor |url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2011/jun/17/new-collected-poems-tomas-transtromer-review |newspaper=[[The Guardian]] |date=17 June 2011 |access-date=6 October 2011}}</ref> in the UK in 2011. He published a short autobiography, ''Minnena ser mig'' ''(The Memories see me)'', in 1993.\n\nBy the mid-1960s, Transtr\u00f6mer became close friends with poet [[Robert Bly]]. The two corresponded frequently, and Bly would translate Transtr\u00f6mer's poems into English. In 2001 [[Bonnier Group|Bonniers]], Transtr\u00f6mer's publisher, released ''Air Mail'', a work consisting of Transtr\u00f6mer's and Bly's day-to-day correspondence on personal, contemporary and literary matters c. 1965\u20131991 \u2013 in a style that vividly conveyed how close friends the two had soon become.<ref name=\"ap nobel\"/> Bly also helped arrange readings for his fellow poet in America. The Syrian poet [[Adunis]] helped spread Transtr\u00f6mer's fame in the [[Arab world]], accompanying him on reading tours.<ref>{{Cite news | url = http://english.ahram.org.eg/~/NewsContentP/18/23495/Books/Adonis-Transtromer-is-deeply-rooted-in-the-land-of.aspx | title = Adonis: Transtromer is deeply rooted in the land of poetry | work = [[Al-Ahram]] | date =6 October 2011 | access-date = 6 October 2011 }}</ref>\n\n[[File:Tomas Transtromer and Modhir Ahmed.jpg|thumb|right|Transtr\u00f6mer (right) with Iraqi-Swedish artist [[Modhir Ahmed]], 2007]]\n\nIn the 1970s, other poets accused Transtr\u00f6mer of being detached from his own age, since he did not deal overtly with social and political issues in his poems and novels. His work, though, lies within and further develops the [[Modernist]] and [[Expressionist]]/[[Surrealist]] language of 20th-century poetry; his clear, seemingly simple pictures from everyday life and nature in particular reveals a mystic insight to the universal aspects of the human mind. A poem of his was read at [[Anna Lindh]]'s memorial service in 2003.<ref name=\"bbc nobel\">{{cite news |title=Swedish poet Transtroemer wins Nobel Literature Prize |url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/entertainment-arts-15200547 |newspaper=[[BBC News]] |date=6 October 2011 |access-date=6 October 2011}}</ref>\n\nTranstr\u00f6mer went to [[Bhopal]] immediately after the [[Bhopal disaster|gas tragedy]] in 1984, and alongside Indian poets such as [[K. Satchidanandan]], took part in a poetry reading session outside the plant.<ref>{{Cite news | url = http://articles.timesofindia.indiatimes.com/2011-10-07/india/30257336_1_swedish-poet-tomas-transtromer-nobel-laureate-nobel-prize | archive-url = https://archive.today/20120714191026/http://articles.timesofindia.indiatimes.com/2011-10-07/india/30257336_1_swedish-poet-tomas-transtromer-nobel-laureate-nobel-prize | url-status = dead | archive-date = 14 July 2012 | title = Nobel laureate has an India connection | work = [[The Times of India]] | date =7 October 2011 | access-date = 7 October 2011 }}</ref>\n\nTranstr\u00f6mer suffered a [[stroke]] in 1990 that left him partially paralyzed and unable to speak; however, he continued to write and publish poetry through the early 2000s. One of his final original volumes of poetry, ''Den stora g\u00e5tan'', was published in 2004, translated into English in 2006 as ''[[The Great Enigma]]''.\n\n===Music===\nTranstr\u00f6mer played the piano throughout his life; after his stroke, which paralyzed the right side of his body, he taught himself to play only with his left hand. He often said that the playing was a way for him to continue living after the stroke.<ref name=\"guardian\"/><ref>[http://www.poetryfoundation.org/harriet/2011/11/tomas-transtromer-plays-piano-in-new-short-doc-on-new-official-website/ Poetry Foundation \"Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer Plays Piano in New Short Doc on New Official Website\" 1 November 2011]</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://tomastranstromer.net/music/audio/ |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120118063712/http://tomastranstromer.net/music/audio/ |archive-date=18 January 2012 |title=Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer \u00ab Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer}}</ref><ref>[[Jean-Pierre Thiollet]], ''88 notes pour piano solo'', Neva Editions, 2015, p.154-155. {{ISBN|978-2-3505-5192-0}}</ref>\n\nTranstr\u00f6mer's daughter Emma is a concert singer. In 2011 she released the album ''Dagsmeja'', containing songs based on Transtr\u00f6mer's poems.<ref>{{cite news |title=Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer : fr\u00e5n vaggan till priset |newspaper= Dagens Nyheter |date=7 October 2011 |pages= 66\u201367}}</ref>\n\nMany composers and musicians have worked with his poems. Among these are [[Jan Garbarek]], [[Ulf Grahn]], [[Madeleine Isaksson]], [[Margareta Hallin]], [[Lars Edlund]], [[Sven-David Sandstr\u00f6m]], [[Jan Sandstr\u00f6m (composer)|Jan Sandstr\u00f6m]] and [[Anders Eliasson]].<ref>[https://www.svenskmusik.org/en/search?search-text=Transtr%C3%B6mer Swedish Music Information Centre]</ref>\n\n===Death===\nTranstr\u00f6mer died in Stockholm on 26 March 2015 at 83, less than 3 weeks before his 84th birthday.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Brown|first1=Andrew|title=Swedish Nobel laureate Tomas Transtr\u00f6mer dies aged
